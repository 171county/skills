## Strategic Foundations

### Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) Framework
Customers don't buy products — they "hire" them to accomplish jobs:
- **Functional job**: Core task ("manage my team's tasks")
- **Social job**: How they want to be perceived ("look organized to my CEO")
- **Emotional job**: How they want to feel ("feel in control")

**Key insight**: Map non-consumption. Most growth comes from people who weren't using *anything* before — not from competitor switching. AI's biggest opportunity: jobs that previously couldn't be done at reasonable cost or speed.

**Application**: List "noncustomers" (people who avoid the category entirely) → ask what job they're failing to get done → surface uncontested space where incumbents have structural blind spots.

### Blue Ocean Strategy (ERRC Framework)
| Action | Question | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Eliminate** | What factors the industry takes for granted? | Reduce cost structure |
| **Reduce** | What should be reduced below industry standard? | Lower complexity |
| **Raise** | What should be raised above industry standard? | Increase value |
| **Create** | What has never been offered? | Generate new demand |

**AI ERRC application**: AI dramatically lowers cost of previously expensive capabilities. Eliminate: large customer support teams. Reduce: implementation time. Raise: personalization depth. Create: always-available expert advice.

### Platform vs. Product Strategy
- **Start as product** unless you're building infrastructure (payment rails, communications)
- **Evolve to platform** when: multiple use cases emerge organically, API usage is high, customers request integrations at scale
- Attempting to build a platform from day one is a common early-stage failure — you must deeply understand the problem before abstracting it

### Network Effects Taxonomy (Andrew Chen)
1. **Engagement Effects**: Product gets stickier as more users join (Slack: more channels = more useful)
2. **Acquisition Effects**: Lower CAC through viral growth (Dropbox: "share folder" → friend joins)
3. **Economic Effects**: Better monetization as network grows (Airbnb: more hosts → better pricing)

**The Atomic Network**: Start with one tightly scoped network that works before scaling (Facebook: Harvard first; Uber: San Francisco only; Slack: individual companies). Must be small enough for density, large enough for value.

**AI data flywheel**: More users → more behavioral data → better predictions → better product → more users. Design data collection and model feedback loops before you have scale, not after.

**Warning**: Data scale has diminishing returns — early points add signal, later ones add noise. Data alone is not a moat. Most durable: data + workflow lock-in + community identity + brand.

## Product Strategy

### Product-Led Growth (PLG) Mechanics
The PLG flywheel:
1. Free experience attracts users (freemium or free trial)
2. Activation — user reaches "aha moment"
3. Habit formation — product integrates into workflow
4. Advocacy — satisfied user shares
5. Network density — more users = more value
6. Revenue expansion — usage growth drives upgrade

**2025 Conversion Benchmarks**:
- Freemium to paid: 2–5% (top quartile: 8–15%)
- Free trial to paid: 14–25% median
- PQL (Product Qualified Lead) to paid: 25–39%
- 97% of buyers prefer to try before they buy

**PQL definition**: Activated user who completed a key action, had their "aha moment," and displayed buying signals (invited teammates, hit usage limits, used paid features in trial).

### GTM Motion Selection by ACV
| ACV | Motion |
|---|---|
| <$5K | PLG-only (product must sell itself) |
| $5K–$25K | PLG with PQL-triggered sales assist |
| $25K–$100K | Hybrid PLG + inside sales |
| $100K+ | Sales-led, enterprise motion |

### Feature Prioritization Frameworks
- **ICE** (Impact × Confidence × Ease): Fast, 1–10 scoring. Use at early stage.
- **RICE** (Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort): Rigorous for scale-stage resource allocation.
- **Kano Model**: Categorize features as Basic needs / Performance features / Delighters / Indifferent. Use in customer interviews to identify what users actually value vs. what they say they want.

### Pricing Strategy Selection
| Model | Best For | Key Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Freemium | High-volume, viral potential | 2–5% conversion; support costs |
| Free Trial | Complex products needing setup | Short window to show value |
| Usage-Based | APIs, AI inference | Revenue volatility |
| Outcome-Based | Agentic AI, vertical automation | Hard to measure; requires eval infrastructure |

**2025 shift**: Outcome-based pricing gaining traction in vertical AI — "5,000 tickets handled monthly" instead of "50 licenses." Sierra and Harvey are pioneering this with strong retention (71% continued value at 6 months vs. 32% horizontal).

## Brand and Creative Strategy

### April Dunford's 5-Component Positioning Framework
1. **Competitive alternatives**: What would customer do if your product didn't exist?
2. **Differentiated capabilities**: What can you do that alternatives cannot?
3. **Differentiated value**: What outcome does that capability enable?
4. **Best-fit customers**: Which customers value this differentiated outcome most?
5. **Market category**: What frame puts your differentiated value in the best light?

**AI startup positioning failures**:
- Competing on "most powerful AI" → leads to commoditization
- Claiming "AI-powered" as differentiation → table stakes by 2025
- Positioning against other AI tools instead of against the manual status quo

### Category Creation vs. Category Entry
- **Category creation**: Define the problem entirely anew. Requires 2–3x more investment in education, 3–5 years to materialize, but category kings capture 76% of market economics.
  - Drift: "Conversational Marketing" — repositioned chat from support utility to revenue tool
  - HubSpot: "Inbound Marketing" — philosophical shift from interruption to attraction
- **Category entry**: Better product in validated market. Faster, lower education cost. Stripe entered after Braintree/Authorize.net; Google after Yahoo/AltaVista.

**Decision rule**: Create a category when you have a proprietary insight about a market that doesn't yet exist AND capital to educate. Enter fast when the problem is validated, incumbent has structural weaknesses, and you can build a meaningfully better product.

### Thought Leadership as Category Moat
Category leaders invest in:
- Original research and data (annual reports, benchmarks, surveys)
- Perspective-driven content (points of view, not just how-tos)
- Defining the vocabulary of the market (when competitors use your language, you've won)

### Creative Differentiation in Commoditized AI
When foundation model capabilities commoditize, differentiation shifts to:
1. **UX depth** — workflow integration depth (Cursor vs. generic coding assistants)
2. **Domain personality** — voice and tone tuned to target user
3. **Proprietary training data** — domain-specific fine-tuning on data competitors can't access
4. **Community identity** — being the tool your target community identifies with
5. **Speed as brand** — Cursor's first moat was purely speed before data or brand advantages

## Competitive Strategy

### Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers
1. Scale economies (cost advantages with size)
2. Network effects (value grows with users)
3. Switching costs (pain of leaving)
4. Counter-positioning (incumbents can't copy without self-harm)
5. Cornered resource (proprietary asset)
6. Process power (unique operational capability)
7. Branding (premium pricing through trust)

### AI-Era Moat Layers (McKinsey Framework)
1. Foundation model access — table stakes, not a moat
2. **Data flywheel** — proprietary behavioral and outcome data that compounds
3. **Workflow integration** — becoming the system of record, making switching catastrophic
4. **Human-in-the-loop trust** — especially in high-stakes domains (legal, medical, financial)

**Most durable combination**: Data flywheel + workflow lock-in + community identity + domain expertise.

### Counter-Positioning (Disruption)
Build products incumbents literally cannot copy without destroying their existing business model:
- Notion disrupted Confluence by making "pages vs. databases" obsolete — Atlassian couldn't replicate without full product rewrite
- Stripe disrupted PayPal through developer experience before any enterprise sales

## 2025 Strategic Themes

### Vertical AI Strategy
- Horizontal AI: Largest/fastest-growing category (5.3× YoY) but commoditized
- **Vertical AI**: $3.5B enterprise spending in 2025 (tripled in one year)
- 3–5× higher retention vs. horizontal; 2.3× higher ROI; 400% YoY revenue growth
- Real moat = industry datasets + deep integrations + codified SOPs — NOT the model
- Target: buyers who already have a budget line for the human doing the job

### Agentic Product Strategy
- Chatbot responds; agent executes
- Design principles: Guardrails over freedom, observability, progressively autonomous, memory architecture
- First company to achieve trusted end-to-end automation in a vertical owns the category

### Incumbent Disruption Patterns (2025–2026)
1. **Workflow automation displacement**: Agent does the job the SaaS tool enabled humans to do
2. **Consolidation displacement**: One AI layer replaces multiple point solutions
3. **Bottom-up displacement**: Individual/developer adopts → company-wide adoption follows

## Innovation Frameworks

### First Principles Thinking
- Break problem to undeniable truths; reason upward ignoring "how things have always been done"
- Musk/SpaceX: $65M rocket → $2M in raw materials = supply chain + reuse opportunity
- Apply to AI strategy: "What is fundamentally true about this job to be done? What assumptions are artifacts of pre-AI tooling constraints?"

### Lean Startup + OKR Integration
- OKRs provide direction (Company Objective → Key Results)
- Lean Startup provides experiment structure (Build-Measure-Learn)
- Every sprint: OKR as anchor, lean experiment as the method, KR as the measurement

### Pivot Theory (Ries' Taxonomy)
Key signals: NPS below 30 after 6 months; CAC exceeding LTV with no reversal path; high trial conversion but immediate churn; sales cycle lengthening not shortening. Pivot is about validated learning, not about whether things are hard.

## Community-Led Growth

### CLG Flywheel
Help → Habit → Advocacy
- **Platform selection**: Discord (developer tools, real-time); Slack (enterprise B2B, high-ACV); GitHub Discussions (open-source)
- **Timeline**: 3–6 months to critical mass (100–500 active members); 12–18 months for measurable business impact
- **Examples**: Supabase ($500M+ built on community obsession); Vercel (Next.js became default React framework through developer ecosystem)
