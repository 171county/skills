## 1. Core Strategy Frameworks

### Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)
Reframes competition: "What job is the customer hiring our product to do?" A job is the progress a person is trying to make in a specific circumstance — not a feature request.

**Three job dimensions:**
- **Functional jobs:** The core task ("get from A to B")
- **Social jobs:** How the customer wants to be perceived ("look competent to my boss")
- **Emotional jobs:** How the customer wants to feel ("feel in control of my finances")

Winning products often dominate on social or emotional dimensions while functionally comparable to competitors. Slack won because it made teams feel more organized and reduced email anxiety, not because chat was technically superior.

**Switch Interview Technique (Bob Moesta):** Interview customers about the moment they decided to switch to your product. Capture the Four Forces: push forces (frustrations with old solution), pull forces (attraction to new), anxieties (concerns about switching), habits (comfort with familiar). The gap between push and pull is your differentiation opportunity.

### Disruptive Innovation (Clayton Christensen)
Two types of disruption:
- **Low-end:** Enter at the bottom with a "good enough" product, improve until mainstream customers switch. Mechanism: incumbents rationally serve their best (upmarket) customers, creating vulnerability from below.
- **New-market:** Create a new market by enabling non-consumption. Example: Airbnb enabling people to monetize spare rooms.

**Important limitations (adversarially verified):** The theory is retrospective, not predictive. Christensen predicted the iPhone would fail. Use for *threat assessment* (am I being disrupted from below?) not for predicting future market winners.

### Blue Ocean Strategy
Create uncontested market space via **value innovation** — simultaneously pursuing differentiation AND lower cost.

**ERRC Grid:**
| Action | Question |
|--------|----------|
| **E**liminate | Which factors the industry takes for granted should be removed? |
| **R**educe | Which factors should be brought well below industry standard? |
| **R**aise | Which factors should be raised well above industry standard? |
| **C**reate | Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered? |

Slack eliminated email chains, reduced formal meetings, raised casual communication speed, created threaded channels.

### Crossing the Chasm (Geoffrey Moore)
Technology adoption lifecycle: Innovators (2.5%) → Early Adopters (13.5%) → **Early Majority (34%)** → Late Majority (34%) → Laggards (16%).

**The Chasm:** Between Early Adopters and Early Majority. Early Adopters don't make credible references for Pragmatists, who want to see other pragmatists endorsing the product.

**Beachhead Strategy:** Pick one specific niche within the Early Majority and dominate it completely before expanding. The bowling pin model: knock over one pin (vertical niche), use it to knock over adjacent pins.

**The "whole product"** must include integrations, training, support, and partner ecosystem — not just the core product — to satisfy pragmatists.

## 2. Product-Market Fit

### Sean Ellis Test
Single question survey to active users: **"How would you feel if you could no longer use [Product]?"**

**Scoring:**
- >40% "very disappointed" → PMF likely achieved, scaling viable
- 25–40% → Approaching PMF, iterate on retention and core loop
- <25% → No PMF, do not scale

**Verified limitations:** False positives documented; 40% is a correlation benchmark, not a universal law. Use as one signal in a multi-signal PMF framework, not a definitive gate. Survey *active users* only; segment results by ICP cohort.

### Retention Curves (Most Reliable PMF Signal)
**Three curve shapes:**
1. **Death curve:** Continuous decline toward zero — no PMF
2. **Smile curve:** Declines, then recovers slightly — possible niche signal
3. **Flattening curve:** Declines initially, then holds at stable level — **strong PMF signal**

**Benchmarks:**
- Consumer apps: 20–40% Day-30 flattening retention is solid
- B2B SaaS: 60–80%+ Day-90 retention is the PMF target

Layer retention curves by acquisition channel, ICP segment, and product version to identify which cohorts retain best.

### NPS Cohort Analysis
Aggregate NPS misleads — segment by ICP vs. non-ICP, time-in-product, acquisition channel, company size. An aggregate +20 NPS might contain a +60 ICP cohort and a -20 non-ICP cohort. Follow low NPS scores with open-ended "why" interviews immediately.

### PMF Signals
**Qualitative (appear first):** Users use product in ways you didn't build for; users are angry when it goes down; users verb your product; organic referrals without incentives; users bring your product into organizations without a sales cycle.

**Quantitative:** Retention curve flattening; 40%+ Sean Ellis score; NPS >+30 in ICP segment; improving CAC:LTV ratio; decreasing time-to-value.

**Rule of thumb:** Qualitative signals appear before quantitative ones. Trust qualitative enough to act, verify with quantitative before scaling spend.

## 3. Go-to-Market Strategy

### ICP Development
ICP is a firmographic/demographic/technographic profile of the company that extracts maximum value from your product AND delivers maximum value to your business.

**Process:**
1. Start with your best 10 customers by LTV, NPS, renewal rate, and expansion revenue
2. Reverse-engineer commonalities: industry, company size, tech stack, org structure, buying triggers
3. Interview them: why did you buy, what problem were you solving, what alternatives did you consider?
4. Build the Negative ICP equally: who should NOT buy (high churn, high support cost, low expansion)
5. Identify trigger events: funding, team growth, compliance change, competitive threat

**ICP → TAM:** SAM = ICP × total companies matching ICP profile × ACV. TAM starts with ICP, not with analyst reports.

### TAM/SAM/SOM Sizing

**Bottom-up (operational planning):**
```
SOM = (ICP companies in market) × (% reachable with current GTM) × (win rate) × (ACV)
```

**Top-down (investor narrative):** Industry analyst data carved out by segment. Use for painting the market vision; use bottom-up to prove you understand how to capture it. Never present top-down TAM without bottom-up validation.

### GTM Motions
**PLG (Product-Led Growth):** Product is the primary acquisition channel. Best when: product delivers immediate value in minutes, viral/collaborative use cases, low ACV ($0–$50/user/month). Examples: Slack, Figma, Notion.

**SLG (Sales-Led Growth):** AEs drive pipeline. Best when: complex product requiring discovery, high ACV ($10K+/year), buying committee of 3+.

**Marketing-Led Growth:** SEO, content, brand drives inbound. Best when: category awareness is low, long buying cycles.

**CLG (Community-Led Growth):** Community drives acquisition and retention. Best when: developer tools, open-source, horizontal platforms.

**Hybrid is the norm:** Most mature companies layer motions. Common pattern: PLG for bottoms-up adoption → SLG for enterprise expansion (Atlassian, Zoom playbook).

## 4. Competitive Positioning

### Positioning Maps
1. Identify 2 key axes that matter to customers
2. Plot all competitors + your position
3. Identify whitespace: customer demand without a credible occupant
4. Validate axes through customer interviews — don't assume which dimensions matter

**Ladder of mind:** Each category has a "ladder" of 3–7 rungs. The top brand gets 2× market share of the second. Being first matters more than being best (Hertz, Coca-Cola, Kleenex).

**Repositioning the competition:** If you can't be #1 in an existing category, reframe so the incumbent becomes #2 or irrelevant. Tylenol repositioned aspirin as dangerous for some users.

### Category Creation vs. Category Entry

| | Category Entry | Category Creation |
|---|---|---|
| Approach | Compete in existing market | Define new market |
| Initial revenue | Faster | Slower (requires customer education) |
| Competitive dynamics | Compete on features/price | Compete on narrative |
| Winner's prize | 3rd place at best | Category kings capture 76%+ of category value |
| Examples | Basecamp vs. Asana | Salesforce ("end of software"), HubSpot ("inbound marketing") |

### Category Creation (Play Bigger Framework)
Category kings capture >76% of their category's total market cap. Three steps:
1. **Frame the problem** in language that makes your solution the obvious answer
2. **Name the category** and own the term through content, conferences, analyst relations, PR
3. **Claim the position before others** via a POV (Point of View) document — 10–20 pages defining the problem, naming the category, explaining why now, positioning you as the only credible solution

## 5. Brand Strategy

### Brand Voice
Defined along axes: Register (formal/casual), Energy (energetic/calm), Empathy (warm/authoritative), Wit (humorous/serious).

**Process:**
1. Identify 3–5 brand personality attributes ("direct, curious, warm")
2. For each attribute, create a "this, not that" example
3. Write 10 sample sentences in voice, get team alignment
4. Create voice guide with dos/don'ts by channel

**Strong startup voices:** Mailchimp (friendly, casual, approachable); Stripe (clear, confident, developer-first); Linear (spare, precise, opinionated); Notion (thoughtful, humble, empowering).

### Visual Identity Systems
Components: Logo system (primary, secondary, mark-only variants), color palette (primary for recognition, secondary for support, semantic for states), typography (headline + body + code/mono), iconography system, illustration/photography style, motion/animation principles, layout grid.

**Startup principles:** Color is the highest-recognition element — choose distinctively (avoid blue if all competitors are blue). At Seed, you need logo + color + typography. Design system investment accelerates at Series A–B. Brand guidelines should be a Figma library, not a PDF.

### Naming Frameworks

| Type | Examples | Trademark Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Descriptive ("Email Service") | — | Weak |
| Suggestive (Coppertone) | Hints at benefit | Medium |
| Real word / arbitrary (Apple, Amazon) | Unrelated real word | Strong (high brand-building investment) |
| Coined/invented (Kodak, Google, Spotify) | Made-up word | Very strong |
| Compound/portmanteau (Pinterest, FedEx) | Word fusion | Strong-medium |
| Metaphorical (Nike = goddess of victory) | Evokes concept | Medium-strong |

**Naming process:** Write naming brief → choose naming architecture → generate 40–100 candidates without filtering → screen for distinctiveness/pronounceability/domain → cut to 5 finalists → validate with customer association test → trademark screen (USPTO + global) → register domain + social handles immediately.

## 6. Pricing Strategy

### Value-Based Pricing
Anchors price to economic or emotional value the customer receives, not cost-plus or competitive parity.

**Process:**
1. Quantify value delivered: time saved, revenue generated, cost reduced, risk mitigated
2. Research willingness to pay: Van Westendorp price sensitivity model, Gabor-Granger technique
3. Segment WTP by customer type
4. Identify the value metric: the unit that scales with customer benefit (per seat, per API call, per contact, per GB)
5. Price to capture value without triggering "too expensive" at your ICP

### Freemium Design
Freemium is a GTM model, not a pricing model. The design question: what sits behind the paywall?

**Conversion benchmarks:** B2B SaaS average: 2–5%; top performers: 5–10%; consumer apps: 1–3%.

**Paywall placement:** Hit at the moment of maximum realized value — not too early (before value is clear), not too late (after value is fully captured free).

**Freemium types:**
- Feature gating: basic free, advanced paid (Trello, Notion)
- Usage gating: free up to X events/API calls/seats (Mailchimp, Mixpanel)
- Network/collaboration gating: free for 1 user, paid for teams (Figma, Loom)

**When freemium works:** Strong network effects, low marginal cost of free users, collaborative/social use cases.
**When it doesn't:** High ACV enterprise ($50K+ deals), high support cost per user, complex onboarding.

### Usage-Based Pricing (UBP)
Aligns price with value consumption. Lower entry barrier, natural land-and-expand. Risks: revenue unpredictability, bill shock churn.

### Price Anchoring
Present a high-priced option first to anchor expectations. Classic SaaS tiers: Starter ($49) / Professional ($149) / Enterprise ($499+) — Enterprise makes Professional feel affordable.

## 7. Network Effects

### Network Effects Taxonomy (Andrew Chen)
**Direct (same-side):** Value increases as more users of the same type join. Example: telephone networks, WhatsApp.

**Indirect (cross-side):** Two-sided — more users on one side makes it more valuable for the other side. Example: Uber (more drivers → faster pickups → more riders → more driver revenue).

**Data network effects:** More users → more data → better model → better product → more users. Example: Waze, Spotify. **Important caveat (a16z-verified):** Data moats are weaker than commonly believed. Model performance saturates, and the cost of adding unique data often outpaces its marginal value. Strong data network effects require *unique*, *proprietary* data competitors cannot replicate.

**Platform network effects:** Developers build on the platform → more functionality → more users → more developers. Example: iOS App Store, Salesforce AppExchange.

### The Cold Start Problem
Every networked product starts with zero users = zero network value. Solution: **The Atomic Network** — the smallest, densest group of users that creates a self-sustaining network.

**Examples:** Uber: one city at a time (dense supply/demand in SF before expanding); Zoom: one team at a time; Slack: one department (engineering) → spreads company-wide.

**Marketplace liquidity:** Initially subsidize supply side (harder to grow) to create good buyer experience. If supply grows faster than demand, supply earns less and churns. If demand grows faster, buyers get poor experiences and churn.

## 8. Pivots

### Types of Pivots (Ries Taxonomy)

| Pivot Type | What Changes |
|---|---|
| Customer segment | Same problem/solution, different customer |
| Problem | Same customers, different problem |
| Solution | Same customers and problem, different solution |
| Revenue model | Same everything, different monetization |
| Channel | Same offer, different distribution |
| Technology | Same customers/problem, different tech architecture |

### When to Pivot
- Retention curves flat and declining despite product iterations
- Sales cycles lengthening, not shortening
- CAC rising, not falling
- Core value proposition doesn't resonate in qualitative interviews
- Only customers buying are outliers, not target ICP

### When NOT to Pivot
- Fewer than 50 qualified sales calls (insufficient data)
- Product hasn't been in market 3+ months
- Reacting to a single customer conversation
- Team morale is low and you're pivoting to relieve pressure

### Investor Communication During Pivot
1. Tell investors before the pivot is public — in a 1:1 call, not an email
2. Frame: What we learned, what we're changing, what we're NOT changing (vision usually stays)
3. Show the data: retention, customer interviews, what forced the change
4. Present the new hypothesis clearly: new ICP, solution, channel — be specific
5. Define success criteria: 90-day metrics for the pivot
6. Increase reporting frequency once pivot is in motion

## 9. Content-Led Growth

### Three Content Growth Motions
1. **SEO-led content:** Own high-intent search queries. Prioritize bottom-of-funnel (product-comparison, use-case keywords) first, then build up to informational/awareness content.

2. **Thought leadership / POV content:** Original research, frameworks, and opinions that change how your category thinks. HubSpot's "State of Marketing" reports (own the data, own the conversation).

3. **Community-generated content:** User-generated posts, how-to guides. Lower cost, high authenticity. Requires community infrastructure.

**Content-to-PMF signal:** When customers start linking to your content in their own writing, when prospects arrive at sales calls already aligned to your terminology, when your framework language becomes industry vocabulary.

### Developer Relations (DevRel)
For products where developers are buyer, influencer, or primary user.

**DevRel goals:** Trust and credibility (developers smell marketing inauthenticity immediately), education (docs, tutorials, SDKs), community (forums, Discord, GitHub Discussions), product feedback loop.

**Content for developers:** Detailed "getting started" guides (first 15 min to value), complete API reference, changelog with explanations of *why* changes were made, engineering blog with genuine technical depth.

**Metrics:** API calls/month, active developers, SDK downloads, time-to-first-success.

### Community-Led Growth (CLG)
**CLG flywheel:** Members join → get value → create value for others → community grows → more members join.

**CLG principles:**
1. Value before extraction — genuine value before any monetization
2. Identity — great communities give members an identity they want to carry ("HubSpot certified expert")
3. Rituals — weekly threads, monthly AMAs, annual events create belonging
4. Reputation systems — badges, titles, leaderboards allow social capital to accrue

**CLG metrics:** Community-sourced pipeline (% new MQLs with community touchpoints), churn rate of community members vs. non-members, expansion revenue from community members.

## 10. Product Strategy

### Build vs. Buy vs. Partner

| Scenario | Best Choice |
|---|---|
| Core differentiator, proprietary advantage | Build |
| Non-core but essential (payments, auth) | Buy/integrate |
| Enter adjacent market quickly | Partner |
| Fast innovation outside core competency | Partner |
| Capability can be commoditized | Buy |

**AI-era rule:** Most "AI features" should be bought (LLM API) rather than built (training foundational models). The differentiation is in the prompt engineering, training data, workflow integration, and trust layer.

### Platform vs. Point Solution
**Land-and-expand platform strategy:**
1. Enter as a point solution (easy to adopt, low friction)
2. Win at that specific use case
3. Add adjacent use cases benefiting from shared data
4. Become "too embedded to rip out"

HubSpot started as a blogging/email tool → CRM → full RevOps platform. 62% of HubSpot deals are multi-hub; ~40% of revenue comes from product lines that didn't exist at IPO.

### Vertical vs. Horizontal SaaS
**Data comparison:**
- Sales & Marketing to Revenue ratio: **19%** (vertical) vs. **41%** (horizontal)
- EBITDA Margins: **13%** (vertical) vs. **0.8%** (horizontal)

**Startup prescription:** Go vertical first. Lower competition, higher ACV, lower CAC, higher switching costs (industry-specific workflows deeply embedded). Expand horizontally once you dominate the vertical.

## 11. Founder-Led Sales

**Why founders must sell first:** Understands ICP, objections, decision process, and language at a depth no briefing can transfer. Sales playbook is written here.

**Minimum viable sales motion (before hiring first rep):**
1. Qualified ICP definition: who should and shouldn't buy
2. Outreach templates: email sequences that got replies
3. Discovery script: questions that reveal buying criteria and urgency
4. Objection handlers: written responses to 8–12 most common objections
5. Proposal/pricing template: consistent value and price presentation
6. Conversion benchmarks: rates at each stage (outreach → call → demo → proposal → close)

**When to hire first sales rep:** After closing 10–20 deals yourself, documenting the process, demonstrating repeatability. Do not hire before repeatability.

**Founder selling advantages:** Customers take meetings with founders they won't take with reps; founders can negotiate terms and make product commitments in real time.

### Founder-Led Marketing
Channels: LinkedIn thought leadership (share frameworks, original observations, honest building stories), podcast appearances, industry event speaking, customer case studies (co-authored), investor content.

**Founder voice principle:** Opinionated, specific, sometimes contrarian. Bland, hedged content disappears. Founders who publicly defend a point of view attract both customers who agree and critics who amplify.

## 12. Startup Storytelling

### Investor Narrative
Messages delivered as narrative are 22× more memorable than facts alone.

**POPP Framework:**
- **P**roblem: "Why should I care?" — make it vivid, real, significant
- **O**pportunity: "Why now?" — market timing, tailwinds, technology shifts
- **P**lan: "How will you do it?" — GTM, business model, moat
- **P**roof: "Why should I believe you?" — traction, team credentials, early signal

**Hearts-Minds-Wallets:**
1. **Hearts (emotion):** Story about a real person experiencing the problem — get emotional buy-in first
2. **Minds (logic):** Market data, product logic, competitive analysis — satisfy the analytical brain
3. **Wallets (action):** Funding ask, use of proceeds, financial projections

**Common mistakes:** Leading with product features before establishing the problem; market sizing without ICP grounding; no "why this team" explanation; weak "why now."

### Customer Narrative
**Hero's journey structure:**
1. The customer before: status quo pain
2. The problem: specific, named obstacle
3. The discovery: how they found your product
4. The transformation: what changed, with specific metrics
5. The new reality: after state

Good narratives: named company + person (if permitted), specific numbers ("reduced processing time by 40%"), a quote expressing emotional satisfaction, before/after contrast.

### Team Narrative
**Three investor questions:**
1. Why are you uniquely qualified to solve this problem? (domain expertise, lived experience, unfair access)
2. Have you worked hard problems together before? (cofounders who've worked together have dramatically lower fallout rates)
3. Do you have the resilience and self-awareness to navigate failure?

**Unfair advantage framing:** Domain expertise ("10 years inside the problem"), network advantage ("access to first 20 enterprise customers"), technical advantage ("hold a patent"), distribution advantage ("500K developer audience as launch channel").

## 13. The Creative-Strategic Synthesis

The most durable startup advantages are built at the intersection of strategic clarity and creative execution.

**Design thinking as competitive advantage:** Apple under Jobs demonstrated that design is strategy. The integration of design thinking means asking not just "does it work?" but "does it feel right?" and "does it reduce cognitive load?"

**Brand narrative as category leverage:** HubSpot invented "inbound marketing" before they had a mature product for it. Salesforce declared "the end of software" before cloud was mainstream. Category creators use narrative to shape the terms on which competition occurs — the highest-leverage creative work a startup can do.

**The creative-strategic flywheel:**
1. Strategy identifies the white space (JTBD + Blue Ocean + ERRC)
2. Narrative claims the white space (category creation)
3. Visual identity and brand voice make the claim memorable
4. Content and community amplify at scale
5. Customer proof validates the claim with evidence
6. Narrative attracts customers → customers generate proof → proof strengthens narrative
