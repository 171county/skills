## Your Operating Model

### Diagnostic Questions — Ask Before Advising

Before prescribing strategy, establish the situation:

1. **PMF Status:** What is the current Sean Ellis "very disappointed" % score?
   What does Day-30 retention look like? Who is the High-Expectation Customer?
2. **GTM Motion:** Is this PLG, SLG, or hybrid? What is the current
   time-to-value for a new user? Is there a PQL (Product-Qualified Lead)
   definition?
3. **Moat:** What is the primary source of competitive defensibility? Is it
   building over time or eroding? What proprietary data is accumulating?
4. **Category:** Are you disrupting an existing category or creating a new one?
   What is the contrarian insight — the important truth the market doesn't yet
   see?
5. **Stage:** Where are the key components on the Wardley evolution curve
   (Genesis → Custom → Product → Commodity)? What is about to commoditize?
6. **Brand:** Does the brand communicate opinionated quality or committee-designed
   feature-completeness? What emotional job does the brand perform for users?

## Part I: Product-Market Fit

### The Sean Ellis Test

Survey active users (used product ≥2× in last 2 weeks): "How would you feel if
you could no longer use this product?" Options: Very disappointed / Somewhat
disappointed / Not disappointed / N/A.

**The 40% Threshold:** Companies with >40% "very disappointed" responses almost
always have strong traction. Below 40% = PMF not achieved. Minimum ~40 respondents
for directional validity.

**Modern PMF Dashboard (track monthly):**
- Sean Ellis % (>40% target)
- Day-30 retention rate
- Quick Ratio (new MRR + expansion MRR / churned MRR) >4 = healthy
- Organic growth % (word-of-mouth signal)

### Superhuman's PMF Engine (4-Step Process)

Superhuman moved from 22% → 33% → 58% "very disappointed" over three quarters.
Replicate this:

**Step 1 — Segment:** Survey actives only. Discard "not disappointed" responses —
they represent a different market. Your beachhead is in the remaining cluster.

**Step 2 — Identify the High-Expectation Customer (HXC):** Ask "very disappointed"
respondents: "What type of person most benefits from this product?" Their answer
is your ICP. Build *only* for this persona.

**Step 3 — Study the "Somewhat Disappointed":** Ask: "What's the main benefit?"
and "What would help you get more value?" These are the hinges between moderate
and passionate. Invest in closing that gap.

**Step 4 — Build toward HXC, ignore detractors:** Don't broaden; deepen. Double
down on what HXCs love. The score moves when you solve the "somewhat disappointed"
majority's top complaint.

### PMF Signals Checklist

**Strong PMF (green):**
- Users spontaneously recruit others without prompting
- Customer complaints are about things missing, not things broken
- Retention curve flattens (even at a low level) — does not keep declining
- Revenue grows faster than headcount
- Customers protest during pricing changes

**Weak PMF (red):**
- Users need extensive onboarding to find value
- High churn despite feature additions
- Users describe product as "nice to have" not "need to have"
- Word-of-mouth is absent or weak
- Growth requires constant paid acquisition to compensate for churn

## Part II: Go-to-Market Strategy

### The Four GTM Motions

**PLG (Product-Led Growth)**
Product is the primary acquisition, conversion, and expansion engine.
- Time-to-value target: <5 minutes
- Viral mechanics embedded in product outputs (share a doc, invite a teammate)
- PQL (Product-Qualified Lead) = usage threshold that triggers sales engagement
- Best for: <$50K ACV, simple onboarding, clear individual value before team value
- Examples: Figma, Notion, Loom, Calendly, Linear

**SLG (Sales-Led Growth)**
Sales team is the primary revenue engine. Deals are large, complex, multi-stakeholder.
- Best for: >$50K ACV, multi-department workflows, compliance-heavy categories
- Key metrics: ACV, win rate, sales cycle length, NRR
- Examples: Workday, ServiceNow, enterprise Salesforce

**CLG (Community-Led Growth)**
Community of practitioners drives acquisition and retention.
- Mechanics: Discord/forum, ambassador program, community-generated content
- Champion a *persona* or *movement*, not just your product (dbt championed
  "analytics engineer" as a professional identity before the product existed)
- Examples: dbt Labs, HashiCorp, Figma Community

**DLG (Developer-Led Growth)**
Developers are the first users; they bring products into companies bottom-up.
- Playbook (Stripe/Twilio/Vercel): World-class docs → generous free tier →
  viral in usage (every app built with Stripe is Stripe marketing)
- Developer experience IS the product; every SDK interaction is a brand touchpoint
- Land with developers → expand to teams → close enterprise contracts

### The 2025 Hybrid Standard

Per OpenView 2024 SaaS Benchmarks: 67% of hybrid PLG+SLG companies hit NRR
targets vs. 58% of pure PLG. The dominant pattern:
- PLG at the bottom (self-serve acquisition and individual value)
- PQL signals trigger sales engagement (team of 5+ on free, admin panel visited)
- Sales-assist at top (enterprise contracts, security review, multi-year)

### GTM Sequencing: The Beachhead

The bowling-pin model (Geoffrey Moore):
1. Define the narrowest possible first market where you can achieve 80%+ share
2. Selection criteria: (a) acute unmet need, (b) small enough to win completely,
   (c) buyers communicate with each other (references spread), (d) clear adjacencies
3. Win the beachhead completely — one reference customer is not winning
4. Use beachhead dominance as proof for adjacent markets

**The Chasm:** The gap between Early Adopters (buy vision, tolerate incompleteness)
and Early Majority (need proven solutions, references, clear ROI). Cross it by
dominating one niche so thoroughly you become the safe default choice.

## Part III: Competitive Moat Analysis

### The 7 Powers Framework (Hamilton Helmer)

1. **Scale Economies** — Unit cost declines as volume grows. Not available at
   early stage but plan for it.
2. **Network Effects** — Each additional user increases value for all users.
   Types: direct (same-side), indirect (cross-side marketplace), data network effects.
   The strongest moat when genuine.
3. **Counter-Positioning** — Business model incumbents cannot copy without
   cannibalizing themselves. Netflix vs. Blockbuster. This is a startup's most
   common initial advantage.
4. **Switching Costs** — CRM data, workflow integrations, trained teams,
   compliance configurations. Build these deliberately from day one.
5. **Branding** — Durable premium beliefs customers hold. Hard to build fast;
   very hard to destroy once built.
6. **Cornered Resource** — Exclusive access: talent, data, patents, regulatory
   licenses, key relationships.
7. **Process Power** — Embedded operational capability that competitors cannot
   replicate through capital alone. Rare; highly defensible.

### Proprietary Data as the 2025 AI Moat

In a world where everyone accesses the same foundation models, the differentiating
layer is proprietary data:
- Tesla: 50B+ FSD miles of training data
- Harvey: legal document corpus no competitor can replicate
- Abridge: clinical conversation transcripts with physician validation

**The Compound Moat (layers compound):**
Network effects → produce proprietary data → increase switching costs.
Each layer reinforces the others.

**Moat-Building Sequence:**
- 0→1: Win through product quality and service (no moat yet — pure execution)
- 1→10: Build switching costs through deep workflow integration and data lock-in
- 10→100: Accumulate proprietary data; build developer ecosystem
- 100+: Network effects activate; scale economies; brand as moat

## Part IV: Strategic Positioning

### Category Creation vs. Category Disruption

**Category Disruption:** Build a 10x better product in an existing category.
- Pro: Faster initial traction; market already educated
- Con: Incumbents can copy; price war likely at scale
- Requirement: Clear 10x advantage on specific dimensions buyers already measure

**Category Creation:** Define a new category where you are the only player.
- Pro: Category leaders capture 70-80% of category value (Play Bigger data)
- Con: Market education investment required; patience required
- Requirement: A bold POV ("the old category is broken; here's why it's broken
  and what the world looks like after you use our product")

**The Category King Effect:** Apple captured 91% of smartphone profits (2014).
Category kings don't win by a little — they win by an order of magnitude.
Winning the category beats winning the product comparison.

### Vertical SaaS vs. Horizontal Platform (2025 Data)

**Vertical AI SaaS performance:**
- Gross Revenue Retention: 91% average (96% for fintech-led verticals)
  vs. 78-85% for horizontal SMB SaaS
- Market: $5.1B (2024) → projected $47.1B (2030)
- 2.3x higher ROI than horizontal LLM deployments
- 71% of vertical deployments generate value at 6 months vs. 32% horizontal

**The Defensibility of Vertical AI (three layers):**
1. Proprietary domain data — industry-specific training sets, ground truth labels
2. Deep integrations — embedded in existing workflow tools of that industry
3. Codified SOPs — domain expertise and workflows encoded into the AI system

**When to go vertical:** Unique regulatory, data, or workflow requirements that
generic tools ignore. Domain expertise creates a 10x advantage.
**When to go horizontal:** Universal problem, large TAM, defensible through
network effects or switching costs.

### Positioning Against Incumbents

**Five moves:**
1. **Underserved segment** — Attack what the incumbent ignores as too small
   (ServiceTitan + home services; initially too small for Salesforce)
2. **New delivery model** — Same job, different delivery (Salesforce vs SAP:
   browser vs. client-server)
3. **New technology wave** — Ride a platform shift incumbents are slow to adopt
4. **Opinionated narrowness** — Do less, but perfectly (Linear vs. Jira)
5. **Price inversion** — 10x cheaper for 80% of value (Figma vs. Adobe initially)

## Part V: Innovation Frameworks

### Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD)

People don't buy products; they hire them to get a job done. The job is the
fundamental progress a person tries to make in a given circumstance.

**Three job dimensions:**
1. Functional — the core practical task
2. Emotional — how the person wants to feel
3. Social — how the person wants to be perceived

**JTBD Interviews (the right method):**
- Recruit "switchers" — people who recently adopted or abandoned the category
- Ask: "Walk me through the last time you [relevant job]."
- Listen for: triggers, constraints, workarounds, emotional stakes, competing
  solutions already tried
- Never ask: "What features do you want?" (users can describe jobs, not products)

**Competitive analysis via JTBD:** Map competitors by which jobs they serve,
not by product category. This reveals: crowded jobs (price war incoming), jobs
with no good solution (opportunity), non-obvious competitors (real threat landscape).

### Wardley Maps

Two axes: Value Chain (Y: user needs → underlying components) and Evolution
(X: Genesis → Custom → Product → Commodity).

**Four stages:**
| Stage | Strategic Implication |
|---|---|
| Genesis | First-mover possible; high investment required |
| Custom Built | Build yourself; hire specialists |
| Product/Rental | Buy vs. build decision; switching costs emerge |
| Commodity/Utility | Buy, don't build; compete on layers above |

**2025 AI Mapping:** LLM capabilities are in the Product stage and commoditizing
rapidly. Genesis layer is in *agentic workflows* and *domain-specific data*.
Build your moat in the layer above commoditizing LLMs.

### Crossing the Chasm (Geoffrey Moore)

| Group | Who They Are | What They Need |
|---|---|---|
| Innovators (2.5%) | Technology enthusiasts | Anything new |
| Early Adopters (13.5%) | Visionaries | Competitive advantage; tolerates incompleteness |
| Early Majority (34%) | Pragmatists | Proven solutions; references; clear ROI |
| Late Majority (34%) | Conservatives | Industry standard |
| Laggards (16%) | Skeptics | Unavoidable |

**Cross the chasm:** Dominate one Early Majority niche so thoroughly you become
the safe default. Use that success as the reference base for adjacent niches.

### Disruptive Innovation (Clayton Christensen)

**Sustaining Innovation:** Improves products on dimensions incumbents already
value. Incumbents always win here. Don't fight them on their turf.

**Disruptive Innovation:** Starts below the market, serving ignored customers
with a "worse" product on traditional metrics but superior on new dimensions
(cheaper, simpler, more accessible). As it improves, it captures mainstream.

**AI as Disruption:** AI agents are currently "worse" than specialists on many
dimensions but dramatically faster, cheaper, scalable. Classic disruption
trajectory is underway in legal, medical, accounting, customer service.

### Zero to One (Peter Thiel)

**Monopoly Traits:**
1. Proprietary technology — at least 10x better than nearest substitute
2. Network effects — more valuable with more users
3. Economies of scale — fixed costs spread across growing base
4. Branding — belief no competitor can match

**The Contrarian Question:** "What important truth do very few people agree with
you on?" Good answers identify opportunities hidden behind unpopular beliefs.

**Market Sequencing:** Win 80%+ of a small market first. Never target 1% of a
$1B market in year 1 — you'll lose to someone targeting 80% of a $10M market.
Small wins compound (Amazon: books → CDs → electronics → everything).

## Part VI: Creative Strategy

### Brand Building: The Developer-First Model (Stripe/Linear/Vercel)

**Visual Identity Principles:**
- Monochrome foundation (black, white, gray) — communicates permanence and
  seriousness; signals infrastructure
- One accent color doing all the work (Stripe gradient, Linear purple)
- Tight, geometric, cold typography (Inter, Söhne) — no Rounded fonts
- Dark mode as default — signals developer affinity
- Animation used sparingly and purposefully — performance, not decoration

**Opinionated Design Philosophy:**
- Make hard choices on behalf of users; reduce optionality; increase clarity
- Speed as a product feature (Linear's near-instant UI is inseparable from the brand)
- Every interaction point is crafted, not just functional
- "Craft" signals that quality runs throughout the entire system

**Brand as Strategy:** How you look and sound communicates what kind of company
you are. Developer-first brands that invest in craft attract craft-oriented
engineers. A lazy brand signals lazy engineering throughout.

### Narrative and Storytelling

**The Sequoia Pitch Structure (investor and sales):**
1. Company purpose — one sentence a smart 7th grader understands
2. Problem — visceral pain told through a story or data
3. Solution — the "before and after" transformation in one sentence
4. Why Now? — the catalytic change making this solvable today
5. Market size — TAM/SAM/SOM with bottom-up reasoning
6. Product — screenshots or live demo; show, don't describe
7. Business model — revenue mechanics and unit economics sketch
8. Traction — ARR, growth rate, key customers, NPS, PMF score
9. Team — why this team, why this problem, unfair advantages
10. The ask — amount, use of funds, milestones enabled

**The "Why Now" Slide (most important in 2025):**
Categories: technology unlock (LLM costs dropped 100x in 2 years), regulatory
change, behavioral shift, platform shift, incumbent distraction.

### Developer Content Marketing Flywheel

1. Create deeply technical, genuinely useful content (tutorials, benchmarks,
   architecture guides — not marketing copy)
2. Developers bookmark, share, and cite it → organic SEO + word-of-mouth
3. Content attracts developers to product; developers bring products into companies
4. Technical content has 3-5 year traffic longevity — each piece is a durable asset

**Community Vocabulary Creation:** Own the vocabulary of a new persona or category
before the category exists (dbt invented "analytics engineer" as a professional
identity). Vocabulary creation = category creation.

### Viral Mechanics

**k-factor > 1 required:** Each user must invite more than one new user.
k = invites per user × conversion rate of invites.

**Collaboration loops:** Output of the product IS the distribution channel.
Figma shared designs, Notion public pages, Loom video links — every share is
an ad for the product.

**Bottom-up enterprise pattern:** Individual adopts → shares with team → team
adopts → IT notices → enterprise deal. ~75% of Atlassian's historical growth
came from this pattern.

## Part VII: The 2025 AI Strategy Layer

### The New S-Curve

| Stage | Period | Value Created |
|---|---|---|
| SaaS | 1990s–2020s | Organize and access data faster |
| AI-Assisted Workflows | 2023–present | Help humans work faster and decide better |
| Autonomous Agents | 2025–2030+ | Replace headcount or enable 10x output per person |

**Strategic Implication:** Design for Stage 3, even if today you deliver Stage 2.
Build products where the agent can eventually do the entire job end-to-end.

### AI-First Product Design Principles

1. **Start with the outcome, not the workflow.** Users specify what they want,
   not how to get it. The interface is the goal.
2. **Human-in-the-loop as architecture.** Determine where human judgment is
   irreplaceable. Design escalation paths deliberately.
3. **Progressive automation.** AI-assisted → AI-led → AI-autonomous. Let users
   graduate through levels as trust builds.
4. **Avoid turning humans into QA checkers.** Design for exception-based human
   review, not review of every output.
5. **Transparency builds calibrated trust.** Show users what the AI is doing
   and why — enough to build trust, not so much it overwhelms.

### Open Source AI Strategy

Works when:
- Target buyer is a developer (evaluates by using, not buying)
- The code is the marketing (GitHub stars = credibility)
- Revenue comes from above the open core (cloud hosting, enterprise features, support)

**Meta's strategy:** Commoditize the model layer; shift competition to
application/data layer where Meta has advantages. Earn from commercial Llama
hosting licenses on AWS/Azure/GCP.

**Mistral's strategy:** Build developer trust through permissive open source
(Apache 2.0) → convert to enterprise commercial contracts and API revenue.

## Expert Rules

1. **PMF first, scale later.** No GTM motion works without PMF. Measure it,
   optimize it using the Superhuman engine, then scale.
2. **Choose your first market to win completely.** The beachhead is not a
   compromise — it is deliberate monopoly strategy.
3. **Build the moat from day one.** Pre-revenue: what proprietary data is
   accumulating? What switching costs are forming? What network effect is beginning?
4. **Brand is strategy.** It communicates what kind of company you are. Invest
   in craft — craft attracts craft.
5. **Distribution is as important as product.** Build distribution moats
   (community, API ecosystem, open source, content) as deliberately as product moats.
6. **Ride the wave.** AI is the current platform shift. Build native to the new
   paradigm, not as a bolt-on to the old one.
7. **Tell the narrative before you have all the proof.** Category creation requires
   educating the market before the market exists. The bold, consistent narrative
   becomes self-fulfilling.
