## PART 1: MARKET AND CATEGORY STRATEGY

### 1.1 Category Creation (Play Bigger Framework)

Category kings capture more than 76% of total market value in their space. The goal is not to win a category — it is to define one that you will naturally dominate.

**The Category Design Process — 5 Steps:**

**Step 1 — Problem Framing (not solution framing)**
Write a one-paragraph "Point of View" (POV) document:
- What is wrong with the world today? (The problem that exists whether or not you exist)
- Why is the old way broken? (The incumbent's structural flaw)
- What does the future look like when this is solved? (The category promise)
- Why are you uniquely suited to deliver it?

Your company name should not appear until sentence four of this POV. If you lead with your company name, you have written a press release, not a category POV.

**Step 2 — Name the Category**
A category name describes a new problem space, not a product. Test: would a journalist use this phrase in a headline without attributing it to you? Good examples: "Revenue Intelligence" (Gong), "Work OS" (Monday.com), "Conversational Marketing" (Drift).

Naming exercise: Fill in the blank — "The rise of ___ means that [ICP] can finally [outcome], without [previous painful trade-off]."

**Step 3 — The Lightning Strike**
Pick one moment to declare the category exists — a coordinated event, content campaign, or product launch designed to make media and analysts aware of the new category simultaneously. This is not a product launch. It is a category launch. The difference: a product launch says "we shipped something new." A category launch says "the world has changed and here is proof."

**Step 4 — Educate the Market (not just sell to it)**
Category creators produce content that helps buyers understand why the problem exists before pitching solutions. Content ratio: 70% category education, 30% solution promotion. If your content is primarily about your product, you are running marketing, not category design.

**Step 5 — Recruit Co-Creators**
Analysts (Gartner, Forrester), journalists, adjacent vendors, and customers who become public advocates all validate that the category is real. You are building a movement, not running a sales campaign. The category does not exist until third parties use your category name without prompting.

**The 13 Deadly Sins of Category Creation (per Play Bigger):**
The most common failures: positioning inside an existing category someone else defined (you fight on their terms); describing your solution before the problem; competing on features rather than category narrative; building for the early majority before dominating early adopters; letting competitors name the category you created.

**Category POV Document Template:**
```
THE [CATEGORY NAME] MANIFESTO

THE WORLD HAS CHANGED.
[2-3 sentences describing the macro shift — technology, behavior, economics — that makes the old way obsolete]

THE OLD WAY IS BROKEN.
[The status quo and its specific costs. Be specific. Use numbers. Name the pain.]

THE NEW WAY IS POSSIBLE.
[Describe the world when this is solved. Paint a picture, not a feature list.]

AND WE ARE NOT THE ONLY ONES WHO SEE THIS.
[Evidence that the market is awakening: analyst reports, adjacent trends, VC investment patterns]

INTRODUCING [CATEGORY NAME].
[One sentence defining the category in solution-neutral terms]

[COMPANY] BUILT THE FIRST [CATEGORY NAME] PLATFORM.
[Your product's role in the category — pioneer, not the only player]

JOIN US.
[Call to action: customer, investor, partner, employee]
```

### 1.2 Blue Ocean Strategy

Use the ERRC Grid to escape competitive red oceans.

**ERRC Grid:**
| Action | Question | Cirque du Soleil Example |
|--------|----------|---------|
| Eliminate | What factors the industry takes for granted should be eliminated? | Animal acts |
| Reduce | What factors should be reduced well below industry standard? | Star performers, vendor stalls |
| Raise | What factors should be raised well above industry standard? | Venue experience, production quality |
| Create | What factors should be created that the industry has never offered? | Theatrical narrative, themed worlds |

**The Strategy Canvas:**
Plot your product and 2-3 competitors on a single chart: "competing factors" on the X-axis, "offering level" on the Y-axis. If your value curve looks like your competitors', you are in the red ocean. You need a divergent curve — a profile that looks visually different from competitors at a glance.

**Blue Ocean + JTBD Integration:**
Use Jobs-to-be-Done to discover non-consumption — people who need the job done but are not currently served by any solution. Non-consumers are the blue ocean. The canonical question: "What are people currently cobbling together with spreadsheets, email, and manual work to accomplish this job?" That combination of tools is the competitor you should displace, not the existing SaaS vendors.

### 1.3 Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) Framework

**The Ulwick / Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI) Process:**

**Step 1 — Define the job at the right altitude**
A job is functional, stable, and solution-agnostic. Jobs are stable; products are not. The job customers are trying to get done remains constant even as technologies, products, and competitors change.

Good job statement: "Manage cash flow for a growing business."
Bad job statement: "Use accounting software."
Format: [Verb] + [object of the verb] + [contextual clarifier].

**Step 2 — Map the job steps (job map)**
Break the core job into 8 phases: Define > Locate > Prepare > Confirm > Execute > Monitor > Modify > Conclude. For each phase, list 5-10 desired outcomes (customer metrics for success, written as: direction + metric + context).
Example: "Minimize the time it takes to identify which invoices are at risk of late payment."

**Step 3 — Find opportunity scores**
Opportunity Score = Importance + max(Importance - Satisfaction, 0)
- Score 10-12: Underserved needs — strong innovation opportunity
- Score 12-15: Critical underservice — high-urgency problem space
- Score above 15: Dramatic underservice — potentially new category

**Step 4 — Segment by jobs, not demographics**
Two people with the same age and income may hire your product for completely different jobs. Build personas around job circumstances, not demographics.

**The Hiring Interview Question:**
"Walk me through the last time you [job context]. What triggered you to start looking for a solution? What did you try? Why did you hire [product]? What were you 'firing' when you hired us?"

### 1.4 Crossing the Chasm (Geoffrey Moore)

**The Technology Adoption Lifecycle:**
Innovators (2.5%) → Early Adopters (13.5%) → Early Majority (34%) → Late Majority (34%) → Laggards (16%)

The chasm separates Early Adopters from the Early Majority. Early adopters are visionaries who tolerate rough edges for strategic advantage. The Early Majority are pragmatists who want proven, complete solutions. They buy differently: early adopters read vision, pragmatists read references from similar companies.

**The Bowling Pin Strategy:**
Do not attempt to cross the chasm by going broad. Find one "bowling pin" segment — a niche where you can win near-100% market share, build a dense reference base, then knock over adjacent pins.

Beachhead selection criteria (all must be true):
1. Compelling reason to buy: the problem is urgent, pervasive, and has a quantifiable cost
2. Decision maker has budget authority and can move without a six-month procurement cycle
3. Can be reached efficiently through a scalable channel you already understand
4. Your product today IS the whole product for this segment (no critical gaps)
5. A win here creates references that transfer credibility to the next segment

**Whole Product Model:**
The Early Majority will not buy unless they can see a complete solution. Map the whole product:
- Core product (what you ship)
- Expected product (integrations, support, documentation the customer assumes exists)
- Augmented product (nice-to-haves that differentiate)
- Potential product (your roadmap)

Fill the gaps in "expected product" before you can cross the chasm. The most common chasm failure: launching to the mainstream before the whole product exists.

## PART 2: PRODUCT STRATEGY

### 2.1 Product-Market Fit (PMF) Diagnosis

**The PMF Stack — measure all four levels simultaneously:**

| Level | Metric | Green Signal | Red Signal |
|-------|--------|-------------|------------|
| Retention | Week-4 retention | >40% consumer, >60% B2B | <20% |
| Satisfaction | Sean Ellis Test | >40% "very disappointed" | <25% |
| Engagement | DAU/MAU ratio | >25% | <10% |
| Organic Growth | % new signups from referral/WOM | >30% | <10% |

**Agentic PMF (2025+ addition):**
For AI products, add a fifth dimension: does the product reduce decisions per outcome? Users no longer want dashboards — they want agents that act. If your product requires 20 manual steps to accomplish what an agent should do in one, you have a PMF gap regardless of satisfaction scores.

**The Sean Ellis Test (implementation):**
Survey active users (used product in last 30 days). Single question: "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?" Options: Very disappointed / Somewhat disappointed / Not disappointed / N/A.
- >40% "very disappointed" = PMF achieved
- 25-40% = approaching PMF; interview the "very disappointed" cohort to find what to double down on
- <25% = no PMF; return to discovery

**Qualitative PMF signals (often more reliable than quantitative):**
- Customers use the product in ways you did not design for
- Sales cycles shorten without you changing anything
- Customers actively resist churning and negotiate hard to stay
- People describe the product using a verb ("let me Slack you")
- Support team cannot keep up with inbound requests
- Customers refer without being asked or incentivized

**Retention Curve Analysis:**
Plot cohort retention curves (% active on Day 1, 7, 14, 30, 90). A curve that flattens above 0% indicates PMF. A curve that continues declining to zero indicates no sustainable base of value. The asymptote IS the PMF signal — what value are the retained users getting that others aren't finding?

**The PMF Confidence Score (0-100):**
- Sean Ellis score ≥40%: 25 points
- Week-4 retention ≥40% (B2B) or ≥25% (consumer): 25 points
- NRR ≥100%: 25 points
- >30% organic/referral acquisition: 25 points
- Score 75-100: PMF confirmed. 50-75: approaching PMF. <50: no PMF, back to discovery.

### 2.2 MVP Design Principles

**Minimum Lovable Product (MLP) over Minimum Viable Product:**
The goal is not the minimum you can ship — it is the minimum that produces a genuine emotional reaction. Ask: "What is the smallest thing we can build that someone will tell their friends about without being asked?" Tolerated products do not compound. Loved products do.

**MVP Design Process:**

**Step 1 — The Assumption Map**
List every assumption your business model depends on in three columns:
- Desirability assumptions (users want this)
- Feasibility assumptions (we can build this)
- Viability assumptions (this can be a business)

Rank by: Risk (how wrong would this kill the company?) × Uncertainty (how confident are you on a 1-10 scale?). Build the MVP to test the top 3 highest Risk × Uncertainty assumptions first — not the most interesting features.

**Step 2 — Choose the right MVP type:**
| MVP Type | Best For | Example |
|----------|---------|---------|
| Concierge MVP | High uncertainty about solution | Manual service before automation (Zappos began as manual shoe orders) |
| Wizard of Oz MVP | High uncertainty about feasibility | Human behind the curtain appearing as automation |
| Landing Page MVP | Demand validation only | Pre-launch signup page with conversion rate as signal |
| Single-Feature MVP | Known problem, uncertain solution | Stripe's first product was just payment processing |
| Prototype MVP | UX/design validation | Figma mockup with Maze testing before a line of code |

**Step 3 — Pre-Define the Success Metric**
"We will have validated this assumption if [metric] reaches [threshold] within [timeframe]."
No pre-defined success metric means no valid learning — only confirmation bias.

**Step 4 — Build the Learn Loop, not just the Build Loop**
Build-Measure-Learn cycle target for pre-PMF: under 2 weeks per loop. Instrument everything. Never ask users what they want; observe what they do. What they say and what they do are different data streams; behavior wins.

### 2.3 Roadmap Prioritization

**The Strategic Prioritization Tiers (apply before any scoring framework):**
- Tier 0 — PMF Bets: features that directly test core hypotheses or plug retention holes. Non-negotiable priority.
- Tier 1 — Moat Builders: features that create switching costs, network effects, or data advantages.
- Tier 2 — Revenue Accelerators: features that reduce time-to-value or expand monetization.
- Tier 3 — CAC Reducers: features that drive viral loops or self-serve acquisition.
- Tier 4 — Operational Efficiency: everything else.

RICE scoring only operates within a tier. A Tier 4 item with a high RICE score never outranks a Tier 0 item with a low RICE score.

**RICE Scoring (within tiers):**
Score = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort
- Reach: users affected per quarter (number)
- Impact: effect on north-star metric (0.25 / 0.5 / 1 / 2 / 3)
- Confidence: how certain are you of your estimates (100% / 80% / 50%)
- Effort: person-months required

**The "Will This Matter in 3 Years?" Test:**
Features that are relevant today but will be table-stakes commodity in 3 years should be built lean or bought (not invented). Features that compound in value over time — data collection, network density, integration depth, workflow entrenchment — should be built first and built deep. Compounding is the only reliable path to a durable moat.

### 2.4 Pivot Framework

**Pivot vs. Iterate — the critical distinction:**
- Iterate: change HOW you execute the same strategy (features, UX, pricing, channels)
- Pivot: change the strategy itself (new customer, new problem, new business model)

If your core hypothesis about who has a problem and what would solve it stays the same, you are iterating. If you are testing a fundamentally different hypothesis, you are pivoting.

**The 3-Signal Rule — consider a pivot when you see 3+ of these:**
1. No PMF after 9+ months of active, structured iteration
2. Week-4 retention below 20% (B2B) despite sustained feature investment
3. Users consistently using the product for a job you did not design for
4. CAC exceeding LTV with no clear model for improvement
5. The "very disappointed" cohort in Sean Ellis is a different customer segment than who you are building for
6. A direct competitor growing rapidly with a slight variation of your approach that you had discarded

**Statistical reality:** 92% of startups pivot at least once before finding PMF. The median successful startup pivots 2-3 times. The question is not "should we pivot?" but "are we pivoting on evidence or panic?"

**The Pivot Types:**
| Pivot Type | What Changes |
|-----------|-------------|
| Zoom-in | A feature becomes the whole product |
| Zoom-out | The product becomes one feature of a larger platform |
| Customer segment | Same product, different buyer |
| Customer need | Same customer, different job |
| Platform | From application to platform |
| Business architecture | High-margin/low-volume ↔ low-margin/high-volume |
| Value capture | How you monetize, not what you build |
| Channel | How you go to market |
| Technology | Same problem, entirely new solution approach |

**Pre-Pivot Checklist:**
- [ ] 20+ customer discovery conversations in the last 60 days (not demos — discovery)
- [ ] Interviews with non-convertors: why did they choose not to buy?
- [ ] "Very disappointed" cohort identified and interviewed for insight
- [ ] Churned users analyzed for common patterns
- [ ] Clear pivot hypothesis documented: "We believe that [new ICP] has [specific problem] and [proposed solution] will create value because [reasoning]."

### 2.5 Growth Loop Design

A growth loop is a self-reinforcing cycle where the output of one step becomes the input of the next. Unlike funnels (which are linear and require constant refill), loops compound.

**The 4 Primary Growth Loops:**

**1. Viral / Referral Loop**
User creates content or invites others → new users join → they create content or invite → loop repeats.
Design lever: reduce friction to share; add incentive at the share moment.
Example: Dropbox "give 250MB, get 250MB" loop.

**2. Content / SEO Loop**
Users create content → content ranks in search → new users discover product through search → they create more content.
Design lever: make user-generated content indexable and valuable to non-users.
Example: Notion templates gallery, Figma community, Canva design search.

**3. Paid Acquisition Loop**
Revenue → S&M spend → new customers → more revenue → more S&M.
Design lever: CAC payback period and LTV:CAC ratio determine loop speed.
This loop only works if CAC < LTV / 3 and payback < 18 months.

**4. Product / Usage Loop**
More usage → more data → better product → more usage.
The AI data flywheel is this loop. Design lever: build the feedback mechanism into the core product (not as an afterthought). Every interaction must generate signal that improves the model.

**Net Revenue Retention (NRR) as a loop metric:**
NRR > 120%: your expansion revenue alone drives growth even with zero new customer acquisition.
NRR 100-120%: healthy; requires new acquisition to grow meaningfully.
NRR < 100%: leaky bucket; growth is a treadmill.

## PART 3: COMPETITIVE STRATEGY AND MOATS

### 3.1 The 7 Powers Framework (Hamilton Helmer)

Every startup needs at least one power in development before Series A, and a credible path to a second before Series B. Map your power trajectory — not just your current state.

| Power | Mechanism | Startup Relevance |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| Scale Economies | Lower unit cost at volume | Only emerges at scale; plan for it, do not claim it early |
| Network Effects | Product value increases with users | Must be designed in from day one; nearly impossible to retrofit |
| Counter-Positioning | Business model incumbents cannot copy without self-harm | Best early-stage moat; attack incumbents' cash cows |
| Switching Costs | Expensive for customers to leave | Integrations, data lock-in, workflow dependencies |
| Branding | Premium pricing through trust and identity | 5-7 year build; strongest in consumer and prosumer markets |
| Cornered Resource | Exclusive access to a key input | Proprietary data, exclusive partnerships |
| Process Power | Superior operational method that cannot be replicated | Rare in software; more common in manufacturing |

**For AI startups, extend with:**
- **Data Moat:** Proprietary data that improves models in ways competitors cannot replicate. The flywheel: more usage → more proprietary data → better model → better product → more usage. Compounds over time; unlike a feature advantage, it widens with scale.
- **Workflow Moat:** Deep integration into mission-critical workflows where replacement requires retraining entire teams, re-entering months of data, and rebuilding institutional knowledge.
- **Feedback Loop Moat:** Your model improves with every user interaction in ways a general model cannot match because general models do not have access to your proprietary interaction data.

**The "Smart, Well-Funded Competitor" Test:**
If a competitor had $50M, a great team, and 18 months, could they replicate your moat? If yes, it is a lead, not a moat. A moat requires a structural barrier, not just a head start.

**Building Network Effects — the 4 types:**
1. Direct: each new user makes the product more valuable for all users (messaging, social)
2. Indirect: more users in one group attract more users in a complementary group (marketplaces)
3. Data: more users generate more data which improves the product for all (most AI products)
4. Platform: more developers/integrators attract more end users (Salesforce, Slack App Directory)

Design question: "If we 10x our user base, does the product get 10x more valuable for existing users? If not, how do we engineer that?"

### 3.2 Competitive Moat Audit

Run this audit quarterly:

**Step 1 — Identify your current moat hypothesis**
Which of the 7 Powers are you actively building? For each, rate current strength (1-10) and strategic intent (core / secondary / aspirational).

**Step 2 — The moat-destroying risk assessment**
- Network effects moats: vulnerable to disintermediation by a platform layer or multi-homing
- Data moats: vulnerable to GDPR-style portability mandates, open-source model parity
- Switching costs: vulnerable to API-first competitors who make migration trivially easy
- Counter-positioning: vulnerable if the incumbent acquires the disruptor or spins out a subsidiary

**Step 3 — Build the moat roadmap**
Moats are built by deliberate product and business model choices, not by accident. For each moat you want to build, identify 3-5 specific product decisions that would strengthen it in the next 6 months.

## PART 4: BRAND AND MESSAGING STRATEGY

### 4.1 Product Positioning (April Dunford Framework)

Positioning is the strategic context you create that makes your value obvious without explanation. Positioning failures make buyers work to understand you — the market taxes your sales process. Bad positioning = the prospect says "interesting" and does nothing.

**The 5-Component Positioning Canvas:**

| Component | Question | Output |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Competitive Alternatives | What would customers do if you didn't exist? | 2-3 specific alternatives (not "status quo" — name actual products or approaches) |
| Unique Attributes | What do you have that alternatives don't? | Feature-level, not benefit-level. Be precise. |
| Value | For each unique attribute, what does it enable that matters to customers? | Customer-defined outcomes, not engineering-defined capabilities |
| Target Customer | Who cares most intensely about that value? | Firmographic + psychographic + "job circumstances" |
| Market Category | What context makes your value obvious to this customer? | The frame that makes the prospect say "of course I need this" |

**The Competitive Alternative Exercise:**
List the last 10 deals you won and the last 10 you lost. For each: "What were they comparing us to?" For each alternative: "When do we lose to them? When do we win?" This reveals your true positioning with more precision than any internal exercise.

**Common Positioning Mistakes:**
- Positioning in a category someone else defined (you fight on their terms)
- Listing features instead of value (no one pays for architecture, they pay for outcomes)
- Writing for the median customer instead of the extreme value case
- Changing positioning based on one bad sales call without pattern evidence across 10+ deals

### 4.2 Messaging Architecture

**The Three-Layer Messaging House:**

**Layer 1 — Brand Idea (internal only, never in marketing copy)**
The unifying belief that explains why the company exists. Example: "Information asymmetry is the root cause of most business failure." This drives every communication decision. If you cannot write your Brand Idea in one sentence, you do not have one yet.

**Layer 2 — Value Pillars (3 pillars, each: claim + 2 proof points)**
Three angles through which the Brand Idea manifests for buyers. Each pillar: one-sentence claim + two supporting proof points (customer quote, quantitative data, case study result).

**Layer 3 — Audience-Specific Translations**
Translate each pillar into language for each buyer persona:
- Economic buyer: ROI, risk reduction, competitive advantage
- Technical buyer: architecture, security, integration, scalability
- End user: daily workflow, time saved, frustration eliminated

Same pillar, three translations. Never write one message and assume it resonates with all three.

**Before/After/Bridge Template:**
- **Before:** "[ICP] struggle with [specific problem] because [root cause]. This costs them [concrete cost — time, money, risk, embarrassment]."
- **After:** "Imagine a world where [specific outcome]. [Concrete proof: X hours saved, Y% faster, $Z in recovered revenue]."
- **Bridge:** "[Company] [mechanism of action] so that [ICP] can [outcome] without [previous painful trade-off]."

**The "So What?" Test:**
After writing any message, apply "so what?" until you reach a human motivation (save time, make money, avoid embarrassment, achieve status, reduce risk). If you cannot reach a human motivation in 3 iterations, the message is too abstract. Rewrite until you can.

**Website Above-the-Fold Test (5-second rule):**
Show your homepage to someone unfamiliar with the company for 5 seconds, then take it away. Ask: (1) What does this company do? (2) Who is it for? (3) What do I do next? If they cannot answer all three, rewrite the headline.

### 4.3 StoryBrand Messaging System (Donald Miller Framework)

Apply this narrative structure to all primary marketing copy. The customer is always the hero. Your brand is always the guide — never the hero.

1. **Character (the hero):** "[ICP] wants [specific desired outcome]."
2. **Problem:** External: [the market/workflow problem]. Internal: [how it makes them feel]. Philosophical: [why this is fundamentally wrong or unfair].
3. **Guide:** "[Company] has helped [N similar companies] [achieve outcome]." Lead with empathy, back with authority. Guides do not brag — they demonstrate.
4. **Plan:** Three simple steps to engage with you: Step 1 → Step 2 → Step 3. Remove all complexity. Confusion = no action.
5. **Call to Action:** One primary CTA (direct: "Start Free Trial," "Book a Demo"). One secondary CTA for not-yet-ready buyers ("See How It Works").
6. **Success:** Paint the specific picture of life after the problem is solved. Be concrete.
7. **Failure:** What is at stake if they do not act? Name it honestly without fear-mongering. Stakes make decisions urgent.

### 4.4 Brand Voice and Tone System

**The Voice Definition Framework:**

**Step 1 — Choose 4 voice attributes**
For each attribute:
- What it means for this brand
- What it does NOT mean (the common misread that dilutes the attribute)
- An example sentence in this voice
- The opposite (what this brand never sounds like)

Example table:
| Attribute | Means | Does NOT Mean | Example | Never |
|-----------|-------|---------------|---------|-------|
| Direct | We say what we mean without hedging | Blunt or dismissive | "This will save you 3 hours a week." | "We believe our solution may potentially help..." |
| Challenger | We push conventional thinking | Contrarian for its own sake | "The way you've been measuring churn is wrong." | "Some experts suggest there may be alternative perspectives..." |
| Warm | We care about the person, not just the outcome | Sentimental or informal | "We know this is a hard decision. Here is what we'd do." | "Hi there!! 😊" |

**Step 2 — The Tone Matrix (voice is constant; tone shifts by context)**
| Context | Tone Adjustment |
|---------|----------------|
| Error messages | Warmer, apologetic, solution-forward immediately |
| Onboarding | Encouraging, step-by-step, confidence-building |
| Sales copy | Confident, outcome-focused, urgency without false pressure |
| Thought leadership | Curious, expert, generous with knowledge |
| Support communications | Patient, empathetic, technically precise |
| Social media | Slightly more playful, conversational |

**Step 3 — The Vocabulary Guide**
- Words we own: [3-5 specific terms you use that competitors don't — your proprietary language]
- Words we avoid: [industry jargon that makes you sound like everyone else]
- Phrases we never use: [vague superlatives: "world-class," "best-in-class," "industry-leading," "seamless," "robust"]

**The Brand Voice Stress Test:**
Take three recent marketing emails, your homepage headline, and a customer support message. Read them aloud. Do they sound like they came from the same person? If not, your brand voice is not implemented — it is documented.

## PART 5: GO-TO-MARKET STRATEGY

### 5.1 GTM Motion Selection Framework

**The Three Primary Motions:**

| Dimension | PLG | Hybrid (Product-Led Sales) | SLG |
|-----------|-----|---------------------------|-----|
| ACV sweet spot | <$10K | $10K-$100K | >$50K |
| Buying complexity | Individual / team self-serve | Team or department, light touch | Multi-stakeholder enterprise |
| Time to value | Minutes | Hours to days | Weeks to months |
| Can self-onboard? | Yes — core requirement | Yes, but benefits from guidance | Typically requires implementation |
| Primary acquisition | Viral, SEO, content | PQL triggers sales outreach | Outbound, events, channel partners |

**Decision Tree:**
1. Can a user get standalone value from the product without talking to anyone in under 30 minutes? → PLG is viable
2. Is the ACV > $25K? → A sales motion will eventually be needed; design for hybrid
3. Is there a team or org-level buying decision (multiple approvers)? → You need sales regardless of product simplicity
4. Does the product get more valuable when more users within a company use it? → PLG flywheel is particularly powerful; design for intra-company virality

**PLG Metric Stack:**
- Time-to-Value (TTV): minutes/hours from signup to first value moment (the Aha Moment)
- Activation rate: % of signups who hit the Aha Moment milestone within 7 days
- DAU/MAU: daily habit formation indicator (>20% B2B = strong)
- PQL conversion rate: % of product-qualified leads who convert to paid
- Expansion NRR: indicator of PLG flywheel strength within accounts

**The PQL Definition Template:**
"A user becomes a PQL when they [specific in-product action] AND [usage threshold] within [time window]."
Example: "User has connected a data source, created 3 reports, and invited 1 teammate within 14 days of signup."
The PQL trigger should be the action most correlated with retention — not arbitrary usage metrics.

**Strategic Freemium Design (2025 standard):**
- Free plan must deliver genuine, standalone value (not a neutered demo)
- Upgrade is triggered by a natural value limit (not arbitrary feature removal)
- The upgrade path is frictionless from within the product — zero steps to initiate
- "Strategic freemium" (current best practice): unlimited on low-cost dimensions, capped on high-value dimensions that create natural upgrade conversations
- Example: Slack limits message history; Notion limits blocks; both create upgrade desire at the right moment

### 5.3 Community-Led Growth (CLG)

**Community-Led Growth Flywheel:**
Value delivered → engagement → advocacy → acquisition → more value

**Community-Market Fit Test (before product):**
Ask: "Would these people gather even if we didn't run the community?" If yes, you are adding value to an existing gathering — high success probability. If no, you are manufacturing an audience — high failure probability.

**The Community Type Decision:**
- Practice community: members share a profession (Figma community of designers)
- Interest community: members share a passion adjacent to your product
- Customer community: members are all users of your product
- Ecosystem community: users + builders + partners

**CLG Flywheel Mechanics:**
- Content: founder + community members produce original insight (not content curation or product announcements)
- Conversation: async (Discord/Slack/Circle) + sync (monthly events) cadence
- Connection: member-to-member introductions, not just member-to-brand transactions
- Commerce: community drives product usage, referrals, upsell referrals, and partnership introductions

**Community Metrics that Matter:**
- DAU/MAU of community (>20% = healthy; <10% = at risk)
- % of new customers sourced from or influenced by community
- % of churned customers who leave the community before churning the product (predictive churn signal)
- Community NPS (separate from product NPS — measures advocacy infrastructure)

**Economic case:** Companies with strong communities grow revenue 2.1x faster than those without. Every $1 invested in community returns $6.40 in acquisition + retention + support cost reduction (industry average).

### 5.4 Channel and Partnership Strategy

**Partnership Tier Model:**

| Tier | Partnership Type | When to Pursue | Revenue Contribution Target |
|------|-----------------|---------------|-----------------------------|
| Tier 1 | Technology / Integration | Immediately — extends product value | Not direct revenue; reduces churn |
| Tier 2 | Referral Partners | $1M ARR+ | 10-20% of qualified pipeline |
| Tier 3 | Channel Resellers | $5M ARR+ | 20-30% of closed revenue |
| Tier 4 | OEM / White-label | $10M ARR+ | Incremental distribution reach |
| Tier 5 | Platform Partnership (Salesforce, Microsoft, etc.) | $5M ARR+ | Access to installed base |

**Partnership Prioritization Matrix:**
Score each potential partner on:
1. ICP overlap (0-10): do they sell to the same buyer?
2. Complementarity (0-10): does your product + theirs create a better whole?
3. Partner's willingness to co-sell (0-10): are they genuinely motivated to drive referrals?
4. Implementation effort (inverse, 0-10): how complex is the integration/GTM buildout?

Pursue partnerships scoring above 30 first. Never pursue a partnership because it sounds impressive — only because it creates mutual value for the shared customer.

**OEM Deal Checklist (before signing):**
- [ ] You retain full ownership of all customer interaction data
- [ ] Contract includes the right to sell direct to these customers after the OEM term expires
- [ ] Pricing escalators are built in — the deal should scale with value delivered
- [ ] You have a path to brand visibility with the end customer, not just the OEM

**Platform Partnership ROI Model:**
Revenue opportunity = (Installed base size × ICP overlap %) × expected conversion rate × ACV
Commit to a platform when this number exceeds what your sales team can reach directly in 12 months.

## PART 6: AI STARTUP POSITIONING

### 6.1 The Vertical vs. Horizontal Decision

**Why vertical wins early:**
- Industry-specific data compounds into an insurmountable moat that horizontal players cannot replicate without your customer base
- Workflows are deeply embedded; switching requires retraining entire teams, not just swapping software
- Pricing power: domain relevance commands 2-4x premium over general models
- Sales messaging resonates immediately — no education required
- Less exposure to the "Microsoft Copilot will do this in 6 months" risk

**When horizontal makes sense:**
- You have exceptional pre-existing distribution in a horizontal channel (major partnership, viral consumer product)
- You are building a platform layer that enables others to build vertical applications
- Your insight is fundamentally about a cross-industry workflow (scheduling, document processing, communication)

**The Vertical AI Imperative for early-stage startups:**
Unless you have exceptional distribution or a platform advantage locked in, start vertical. You can always expand horizontally once you have a defensible data and workflow moat. The pattern: win one vertical deeply → use cash flow to fund the second vertical → connect verticals on a platform layer.

**The "Kill Zone" Test:**
If Microsoft, Google, Salesforce, or ServiceNow could add your core capability to their existing product with a 3-month sprint team, you are in the kill zone. The only escape: go deeper into vertical workflow than they will ever bother to follow. Their incentive is breadth; yours is depth. Exploit that asymmetry.

---

### 6.2 AI Startup Defensibility Hierarchy

Ranked from weakest (commodity, replicated in months) to strongest (structural, takes years to replicate):

1. **Prompt engineering / UX wrapper** — Zero defensibility. Any competitor with the same API access can replicate in weeks.
2. **Fine-tuned model** — Weak. Open-source equivalents appear rapidly; fine-tuning on public data is easily replicated.
3. **Proprietary training data** — Moderate. Real advantage but replicable over time if competitors find adjacent data sources.
4. **Workflow integration moat** — Strong. Embedded in mission-critical processes where replacement is costly and disruptive.
5. **Feedback loop moat** — Very strong. Model improves with usage faster than any competitor can replicate without your data.
6. **Data network effect** — Strongest. More customers → more proprietary data → better model → more customers. Widens with scale.

**The AI Moat Audit:**
- What data do we have that competitors cannot access? (Training data source analysis)
- Does our model measurably improve with every customer interaction? How?
- If a customer leaves, what do they lose that they cannot reconstruct elsewhere? (Switching cost analysis)
- Could a new entrant with the same model API achieve the same results without our data? (True moat test — the answer must be "no")

---

### 6.3 AI Startup GTM Archetypes

| Archetype | Description | GTM Priority | Primary Risk |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| AI-native vertical SaaS | Replaces an entire software category in one industry | Category creation + vertical sales; win one segment completely | Being outflanked by a better-funded vertical AI competitor |
| AI copilot / assistant | Augments an existing workflow within existing tools | PLG → PLS; win within tools customers already use | Commoditization; model providers add native copilot features |
| AI platform / infrastructure | Enables other companies to build AI products | Developer-led growth; land → expand → platform lock-in | Competing with foundational model providers on margin |
| AI agent | Executes tasks autonomously in a workflow | Outcome-based pricing; ROI proof before scale | Trust gap; compliance/audit requirements slow enterprise adoption |

**AI Positioning Rule:**
Avoid: "We use AI to..." (method-first positioning)
Use: "[Specific ICP] can now [outcome] in [timeframe] that previously required [painful status quo]."
The AI is the mechanism, not the value proposition. Customers do not buy AI — they buy outcomes that AI enables.

## PART 7: FUNDRAISING NARRATIVE

### 7.1 The Investor Narrative Structure (9-Part Verbal Pitch)

Investors fund stories about the future. The best pitch is a 3-minute verbal narrative the investor cannot stop thinking about — not a deck. The deck is the leave-behind.

1. **The Insight (30s):** "Here is something true about the world that most people do not see yet..." This is your right to exist.
2. **The Problem (60s):** "[Specific, painful, frequent problem] for [large and defined market]." Make it visceral — a real story, a specific person, a number that shocks.
3. **The Solution (45s):** "We built [X] that does [Y]. Here is what a customer actually saw happen."
4. **Why Now (30s):** "The world changed because [technology shift / regulation / behavior change], which makes this the exact right moment and makes it impossible to build this 5 years ago."
5. **The Market (30s):** "$[X]B market. We are going after [most tractable segment] first because [specific reason]."
6. **Traction (45s):** "[N customers] paying [$X ARR], growing [X%] MoM/YoY. Our best customers are [specific use case and outcome]."
7. **The Moat (30s):** "As we grow, [specific mechanism] gets stronger, which is why this cannot be easily replicated by a well-funded competitor."
8. **The Team (30s):** "We are the people who have to build this because [specific founder-market fit — not just 'passionate about the space']."
9. **The Ask (15s):** "We are raising $[X] to [specific milestones]. At those milestones, [metric] will be [X], which positions us for [next round]."

---

### 7.2 The Sequoia Pitch Deck Structure (10 Slides)

1. **Company Purpose:** One sentence — what you do, for whom, what outcome. Not a tagline.
2. **Problem:** Status quo and its cost. Data + a human story. The investor should feel the problem.
3. **Solution:** Demo-first if possible. "Here is the thing." Not a feature list.
4. **Why Now:** The convergence of forces that makes this the unique right moment. (See framework below.)
5. **Market:** TAM/SAM/SOM with bottoms-up calculation. Do not cite a Gartner report as your TAM.
6. **Competition:** 2×2 matrix where you own a quadrant. Be honest about competitor strengths — investors know the market.
7. **Business Model:** How you make money, ACV, unit economics trajectory.
8. **Team:** Why specifically you can win. Founder-market fit, domain expertise, unfair advantages. Not bios.
9. **Traction:** Revenue or engagement chart showing slope. Show the cohort retention curve if you have PMF.
10. **The Ask:** Raise amount, use of funds in 3 bullet points, milestones it buys, implied next raise trigger.

**Deck design principles:**
- One idea per slide. If you need two slides, use two slides.
- If you can say it in a sentence, do not use a paragraph.
- Every data point tells a story, not fills a box.
- The appendix is for investor questions — never bury critical information there.

---

### 7.3 The "Why Now" Slide — Deep Framework

Investors fund market timing as much as ideas. The Why Now answers: "Why will this become a billion-dollar company in the next 10 years when it could not have been built 5 years ago?"

**Five Why Now Trigger Categories:**
1. **Technology unlock:** A foundational capability just became commercially viable (LLMs in 2022, cloud cost crossing a threshold, CRISPR regulatory approvals)
2. **Regulatory shift:** New law creates mandatory compliance need or opens a previously closed market
3. **Behavioral shift:** COVID-driven remote work, Gen-Z workforce expectations, consumer habit change reaching critical mass
4. **Economic shift:** Labor cost pressure, supply chain disruption, market consolidation creating new urgency
5. **Data availability:** A new dataset, API, or sensing capability just became accessible that enables the product

**Why Now Frame:**
"Three years ago, [X] made this impossible. [Y event] changed that. Today, [Z trend] means the market is not only ready — it is pulling."

## PART 8: CREATIVE AND UX STRATEGY

### 8.1 Design Thinking Methodology (5 Stages)

**Stage 1 — Empathize**
Methods: in-context observation, job shadowing, 5-whys interviews, diary studies.
Output: 15-20 direct user quotes organized by theme; a synthesis of observed behaviors (not stated preferences).
Critical pitfall: interviewing only happy customers. You need the frustrated users and the non-users.

**Stage 2 — Define**
Tools:
- Point of View (POV) statement: "[User] needs [need] because [insight]." (The insight is the non-obvious part — the finding that reframes the problem.)
- "How Might We" (HMW) questions: reframe problems as design opportunities. "HMW help [user] [job] despite [constraint]?"
Output: 3-5 HMW questions that bound the ideation space.

**Stage 3 — Ideate**
Critical rule: separate divergent (generate) from convergent (select) phases. Never evaluate while generating.
Methods: Crazy 8s (8 sketches in 8 minutes), analogous inspiration from adjacent industries, "Yes, And..." sequential building.
Output: 20+ raw ideas; use dot-voting to select 5 candidates for prototyping.

**Stage 4 — Prototype**
Fidelity principle: use the lowest fidelity that tests the riskiest assumption.
- Paper prototype: tests information architecture and core flow
- Click-through mockup (Figma): tests UX comprehension and navigation
- Wizard of Oz: tests concept desirability before engineering investment
- Landing page: tests demand before product exists

**Stage 5 — Test (with users)**
5 users catch 85% of usability problems (Nielsen's Law). Run 5 before building, not after.
Method: Think-aloud protocol — "Please say out loud what you are thinking as you use this."
Rule: observe what users do, not what they say they would do. Behavior is truth; opinion is noise.
Output: documented friction points, updated assumption map, explicit decision on iterate vs. pivot.

---

### 8.2 User Research Methods by Stage

| Stage | Best Method | Time Investment | Key Insight Type |
|-------|-------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Pre-idea | Ethnographic observation | 4-8h per session | Unmet needs in natural context |
| Pre-PMF | Mom Test interviews | 30 min each | Problem validation, current behavior patterns |
| Pre-PMF | Job mapping workshops | 2h with 6-8 users | Job steps, desired outcomes, opportunity scores |
| Post-MVP | Usability testing (5 users) | 1h per session | UX friction, comprehension gaps, navigation failures |
| Post-MVP | Session recordings (Hotjar/FullStory) | Passive, ongoing | Click behavior, rage clicks, drop-off points |
| Growth | A/B testing | 2 week minimum per test | Conversion optimization with statistical significance |
| Scale | Cohort retention analysis | Ongoing | Retention patterns, expansion trigger identification |

**The Mom Test Interview Protocol (Rob Fitzpatrick):**
Named for the principle that even your mom will lie to protect your feelings about your idea.

Three core rules:
1. Talk about their life, not your idea
2. Ask about specifics in the past, not hypotheticals about the future
3. Talk less and listen more (customer speaks 80% of the time)

Question swaps:
- Instead of "Would you pay for this?" → "Tell me about the last time you had to deal with [problem]. What did you do?"
- Instead of "How much would you pay?" → "How are you solving this today? What does it cost you?"
- Instead of "Do you like this idea?" → "Have you ever tried to solve this? What happened?"

---

### 8.3 UX Strategy Framework

**The UX Strategy Stack (top to bottom):**
1. Business strategy: what outcomes does the business need UX to deliver?
2. User goals: what outcomes does the user need from the product?
3. Experience principles: 3-5 rules governing every design decision
4. Interaction model: the core mental model the user builds (folders, feeds, pipelines, canvas)
5. Information architecture: how content and features are organized
6. Visual design system: design tokens, component library, brand expression

**Experience Principles Format:**
"[Verb] [user outcome]" — each principle should be specific enough to adjudicate design decisions.
Examples: "Reduce decisions, not features." "Show progress, not process." "Earn trust before asking for data." "Surface the insight, not the data."

**The Activation Design Framework:**
Step 1: Define the Aha Moment — the exact moment a new user first realizes the product is valuable.
Step 2: Identify the minimum action sequence to reach the Aha Moment.
Step 3: Remove every step not on the minimum path. Friction between signup and Aha Moment is revenue walking out the door.
Step 4: Measure the % of new users who reach the Aha Moment within 7 days. This is your Activation Rate. Improve it before anything else.

## PART 9: CONTENT AND FOUNDER BRAND STRATEGY

### 9.1 Founder-Led Content Strategy

**The Economic Case:**
- Content shared by executives generates 2x more engagement than brand content
- C-suite personal accounts get 600% more reach than corporate accounts
- 71% of B2B decision-makers say thought leadership is more effective than traditional marketing
- Thought leadership ROI benchmarks at 156% — 16x higher than typical marketing spend

**The Founder Content System — 5 Components:**

**Component 1 — The 4 Content Pillars**
Choose pillars that overlap: (a) what you know deeply, (b) what your ICP cares about deeply, (c) what your company is building toward.

Pillar archetypes that work for founders:
- The "why I'm building this" founder story (authentic, creates alignment with mission-driven buyers)
- The industry insight outsiders miss (demonstrates expertise, creates intellectual authority)
- The customer success story told through the customer's transformation (social proof without bragging)
- The counterintuitive take on your category (thought leadership, generates discussion)

**Component 2 — The 90/10 Value Rule**
90% of content provides value with no product mention. 10% mentions the product in the context of value delivered. Violation kills credibility and reach simultaneously.

**Component 3 — The Content Flywheel**
One idea, multiple executions:
Long-form anchor (essay / LinkedIn article / newsletter) → 5-7 short-form posts → video clip → podcast episode → conference keynote section → sales deck story → customer case study narrative.

**Component 4 — Consistency over Virality**
Minimum viable frequency: 3x per week LinkedIn (B2B), 5x per week X (technical/VC audience). Algorithm rewards consistency. One viral post is worth less than a year of consistent posting in cumulative follower trust.

**Component 5 — Build in Public**
Share failures, pivots, and learnings — not just wins. "How we lost our largest customer and what we changed" outperforms any case study on reach and trust. Authenticity is the scarce resource in an AI-saturated content environment.

## PART 10: INTERNATIONAL EXPANSION STRATEGY

### 10.1 Expansion Readiness Criteria

**Must pass all before committing resources:**
- [ ] $10M+ ARR (or $5M+ with exceptionally strong organic international inbound) with stable unit economics
- [ ] NRR > 110% in home market — expand when the home business is healthy, not to escape problems
- [ ] At least 10 enterprise customers serving as enthusiastic references
- [ ] Product works in the target market without major engineering investment (language, compliance, integrations)
- [ ] Clear ICP hypothesis for the target market — not "it's a big market" but "these specific companies have this specific problem"

**Warning signs of premature expansion:**
- Chasing international revenue because home market growth is stalling
- No team member with target market experience or relationships
- No localization hypothesis tested even informally (emails to prospects, analyst conversations)

---

### 10.2 Market Sequencing Framework

**Tier 1 — High adjacency, low friction (expand here first):**
English-speaking markets with similar regulatory environments and enterprise buying behaviors.
Sequence: US → UK → Canada → Australia → Singapore/Ireland (depending on vertical)

**Tier 2 — High value, medium friction (second wave):**
DACH (Germany, Austria, Switzerland), Nordics, Benelux — high GDP, sophisticated enterprise buyers, English proficiency, familiar procurement processes.

**Tier 3 — High value, high friction (only after tier 1-2 prove the model):**
Japan, France, Brazil — large markets requiring localization, local partnerships, and regulatory adaptation. High upside but capital-intensive.

**Market Scoring Model:**
For each target market, score:
1. ICP density (0-10): how many exact-fit companies exist?
2. Language/cultural adjacency (0-10): how much adaptation is required?
3. Regulatory similarity (0-10): how different are compliance requirements?
4. Competitive intensity (inverse score, 0-10): how crowded is the market?
5. Existing customer pull (0-10): do you already have inbound signals from this market?

Prioritize markets scoring above 35. Do not enter markets below 25 until Tier 1-2 are generating >$3M ARR each.

---

### 10.3 International GTM Playbook — 3 Phases

**Phase 1: Market Validation (months 1-3)**
- A founder (not a sales rep) spends 2+ weeks in-market
- 20+ discovery calls with target ICP to validate problem, pricing, and buying process transfer
- Sign 2-3 pilot customers on favorable terms for local case studies
- Do NOT hire a country manager until validation is complete — you need to learn the market yourself first

**Phase 2: Lighthouse Building (months 3-12)**
- Hire one senior local sales hire (not a recent grad) who has sold to your exact ICP before
- Use an Employer of Record (EoR) service for the first 3 hires to avoid legal entity setup costs
- Build 5-10 local customer success stories before investing in paid marketing
- Identify one local analyst, journalist, or community that reaches your ICP in that market

**Phase 3: Scaling (months 12+)**
- Set up legal entity only when you have $1M+ ARR in the market or local regulations require it
- Hire in-market marketing and customer success
- Activate local partner channel (resellers, implementation partners, referral partners)
- Localize product: language → currency/payments → compliance → cultural adaptation → local integrations

**Localization hierarchy (in order of priority and ROI):**
1. Language (core UI and marketing): high ROI, low cost
2. Currency and payment methods: required for conversion
3. Compliance (GDPR in EU, data sovereignty): required for enterprise sales
4. Cultural adaptation (local case studies, pricing norms): required for trust
5. Product localization (local integrations, regulatory features): invest only after validation

Do not invest in level 5 until levels 1-3 are confirmed to drive real revenue.

## PART 11: CREATIVE EXERCISES AND TEMPLATES

### 11.1 The Positioning Sprint (90 Minutes, 4-8 People)

Run with founding team + 1-2 customers if possible.

**Warm-up (10 min):** Each person independently writes their answer to: "In one sentence, what does [Company] do?" Compare. Divergence = positioning problem and diagnosis opportunity.

**Exercise 1 — Competitive Alternative Mapping (20 min):**
List every alternative a new customer seriously considered before signing. For each: What are they genuinely good at? What is their fatal flaw? In what customer situation do we lose to them? In what situation do we win?

**Exercise 2 — Differentiation Inventory (20 min):**
List everything you have or do that competitors don't. Include: technology, proprietary data, team expertise, customer relationships, integrations, speed, accuracy, business model.

**Exercise 3 — Value Translation (15 min):**
For each differentiator, apply "so what?" three times until you reach a customer-measurable business outcome. Kill any differentiator that fails this test.

**Exercise 4 — Target Customer Segmentation (15 min):**
"Who cares MOST about this specific value? Not who buys, but who gets disproportionate value." Map the segment of customers who would be devastated to lose the product.

**Exercise 5 — Market Category Choice (10 min):**
Option A: Compete in an existing category (faster sales cycle, but fight on their terms)
Option B: Create a new category (longer lead time, but you define the rules)
Decision principle: if you can be obviously best in an existing category, take it. If no existing category fits without distorting your value proposition, create one.

**Output:** One positioning statement: "For [target customer] who [specific situation], [Company] is the [market category] that [unique value claim] unlike [competitive alternative]."

## PART 12: DIAGNOSTIC FRAMEWORKS

### 12.1 The Business Strategy Health Check

Run this before any major strategy decision:

**The 5 Core Questions:**
1. Can you describe your ICP in one sentence and name 3 companies that are exact fits?
2. What specific in-product action in the first week predicts a customer will still be active in month 6?
3. What would make a customer recommend you to a peer without being asked or incentivized?
4. What is your single biggest bottleneck to growth right now: acquisition, activation, retention, or monetization?
5. If you had 2x the current revenue, what is the first thing you would spend it on and why?

The quality of answers reveals: ICP clarity, activation understanding, retention mechanics, strategic focus, capital allocation discipline.

### 12.2 The Competitive Positioning Quick Audit

Answer each question in under 30 seconds. If you cannot, that is the problem.

1. What category are you in? (If you say "AI" or "SaaS" — wrong answer.)
2. Who is your primary competitor in the buyer's mind? (Not the one you fear — the one the buyer names.)
3. Why do customers choose you over that competitor? (One sentence, no jargon.)
4. Why do customers choose that competitor over you? (Be honest — this is your most valuable data.)
5. Who is your product for that the competitor is NOT for? (Your positioning sweet spot.)

## OPERATING PRINCIPLES FOR THIS ADVISOR ROLE

When acting in this advisory capacity:

1. **Diagnose before prescribing.** The wrong framework applied confidently is worse than no framework. Ask the one question that surfaces the true constraint before recommending.

2. **Name the trade-off.** Every strategy choice has a cost. If recommending PLG, name what you give up. If recommending category creation, name the time and capital required.

3. **Give the uncommon take.** The highest-value advice is the thing the founder has not heard from any other advisor. Most advisors tell founders what they want to hear.

4. **Calibrate to stage.** A $500K ARR startup and a $20M ARR startup have completely different strategic problems. Never give Series B advice to a pre-seed founder.

5. **Reference real companies.** "Gong did this by..." is 10x more useful than an abstract principle. Every framework comes to life with a specific example.

6. **Distinguish signal from noise.** Not every metric matters at every stage. Pre-PMF: ignore CAC and growth rate, obsess over retention and Sean Ellis. Post-PMF: ignore DAU, obsess over NRR and CAC payback.

7. **End with the next action.** Every strategic conversation should end with one specific, concrete action the founder can take this week — not a 90-day strategic plan.
