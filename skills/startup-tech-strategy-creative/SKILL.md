## Expert Mental Model

Strategy and creativity are not opposites — they are the same discipline at different time horizons:
- **Creative** asks: what should exist that doesn't?
- **Strategy** asks: how do we make sure it keeps existing when others try to kill it?

The practitioner loop:
1. **Customer truth** — JTBD interviews reveal what people actually hire your product to do
2. **Competitive map** — 7 Powers analysis reveals where durable advantage is possible
3. **GTM wedge** — identify the narrowest, most defensible beachhead
4. **Brand compression** — compress strategy into 3 words a customer would use to describe you
5. **Narrative construction** — turn strategy into a pitch, a landing page, a sales script
6. **Feedback instrumentation** — measure PMF signal, iterate on positioning, not just product

## 1. Competitive Strategy Foundations

### Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers

The definitive framework for durable competitive advantage. Each Power creates both **benefit** (margin) and **barrier** (prevents competitors from eliminating it).

**1. Scale Economies**
- Benefit: unit cost declines as volume increases
- Barrier: competitors can't match cost structure without matching scale
- Startup signal: gross margin improves predictably with cohort size
- Example: AWS, Stripe's infrastructure economics, Cloudflare CDN

**2. Network Effects**
- Benefit: product value increases with each new user
- Barrier: users won't switch to a smaller network even if product is better
- Types: direct (messaging), indirect (marketplace), data (recommendation engine)
- Startup signal: retention improves as user count grows; cross-side value visible
- Example: Figma (direct), Shopify (indirect marketplace/ecosystem), LinkedIn (direct)

**3. Counter-Positioning**
- Benefit: new business model your incumbent can't copy without damaging their core
- Barrier: incumbent's rational self-interest prevents them from responding
- Startup signal: incumbents attack you with marketing, not product
- Example: Netflix vs. Blockbuster (no late fees), Zoom vs. Cisco WebEx (freemium)
- AI-era signal: LLM-native products that cannibalize incumbent consulting/SaaS revenue

**4. Switching Costs**
- Benefit: customers stay because leaving is painful
- Barrier: switching costs exceed any rational improvement threshold
- Types: financial (migration cost), procedural (workflow disruption), relational (trust)
- Startup signal: net revenue retention (NRR) >110% is early evidence
- Example: Salesforce, Workday, developer tooling (AWS lock-in)

**5. Branding**
- Benefit: customers pay premium based on identity and trust, not just utility
- Barrier: brand takes years to build; can't be purchased or replicated quickly
- Startup signal: customers describe you in emotional terms; NPS from identity not features
- Example: Stripe (developer trust brand), Linear (taste brand), Apple (identity brand)

**6. Cornered Resource**
- Benefit: exclusive access to an asset others can't get
- Barrier: the resource is non-replicable
- Types: patents, exclusive data, unique talent, regulatory licenses
- Startup signal: if you have something competitors categorically cannot access
- Example: Waymo's dataset, OpenAI's GPT-4 exclusivity window, Bloomberg's financial data

**7. Process Power**
- Benefit: embedded operational excellence that compounds over time
- Barrier: process improvements are tacit, not codable, not transferable
- Startup signal: quality improves faster than headcount grows
- Example: Toyota Production System, SpaceX's manufacturing learning curve

**Power sequencing rule (Hamilton Helmer):**
- Origination: Counter-positioning is the most common startup entry point
- Takeoff: Network effects and switching costs emerge during scale
- Stability: Scale economies and branding lock in advantage at maturity

### Blue Ocean Strategy (Kim & Mauborgne)

Use the **Four Actions Framework** to reconstruct market boundaries:
- **Eliminate**: What factors does the industry take for granted that should be eliminated?
- **Reduce**: What factors should be reduced well below the industry standard?
- **Raise**: What factors should be raised well above the industry standard?
- **Create**: What factors should be created that the industry has never offered?

**Strategy Canvas**: Plot your offering vs. competitors across 8-12 competitive factors. Blue Ocean = your curve looks different from everyone else's, not just better on the same axes.

### Porter's Five Forces (for competitive context)

Use to map industry structure before entering:
1. Threat of new entrants (barriers to entry)
2. Bargaining power of suppliers
3. Bargaining power of buyers
4. Threat of substitute products
5. Competitive rivalry

**2025 AI-era addendum**: Factor in "threat of LLM substitution" as a sixth force — any workflow that is primarily information processing is at risk of being automated by foundation models.

## 2. Jobs-to-Be-Done Framework

### Core JTBD Theory (Christensen / Moesta)

Customers don't buy products — they hire them to make progress in their lives. The unit of analysis is the job, not the customer segment.

**Job statement structure**: [Verb] + [Object] + [Context/Constraint]
- Bad: "users want to send messages"
- Good: "help me coordinate my team's status without scheduling another meeting"

**The Four Forces of Progress** (Bob Moesta):
- Push (from current solution): frustration with existing approach
- Pull (toward new solution): appeal of the new possibility
- Anxiety (about new solution): fear of change, risk, unknown
- Habit (of current solution): inertia toward the familiar

**Interview methodology** (JTBD switch interviews):
- Interview people who recently made a purchase decision in your category
- Ask: "Walk me through the day you decided to switch/buy"
- Listen for: first thought, timeline of decision, what they tried first, what surprised them
- Never ask: "What features do you want?" — customers describe solutions, not jobs

**Job map (JTBD** taxonomy):
1. Define → 2. Locate → 3. Prepare → 4. Confirm → 5. Execute → 6. Monitor → 7. Modify → 8. Conclude

**Apply**: Map your product against each step of the job map. Where are you not executing? Where is the competitor executing? Where is no one executing well?

## 3. Go-to-Market Motion Design

### PLG / SLG / MLG Decision Framework

**Product-Led Growth (PLG)**
- Users experience value before sales involvement
- Signals needed: short time-to-value (<5 minutes to "aha"), no procurement required, viral loop possible
- Metrics: Activation rate, PQL (product-qualified lead) conversion, viral coefficient (k-factor)
- Examples: Figma, Notion, Calendly, Vercel, Linear
- Investment: Product team owns GTM; sales enters only at expansion/enterprise
- AI-era note: LLM-powered onboarding eliminates most friction previously requiring human CS

**Sales-Led Growth (SLG)**
- ACV >$30K, multi-stakeholder purchase, compliance/security review required
- Signals: complex integration, IT/security gatekeeping, procurement cycle >30 days
- Metrics: MQL→SAL→SQL→Opp→Close rates, ACV, sales cycle length, CAC payback
- Examples: Workday, Salesforce, enterprise cybersecurity

**Marketing-Led Growth (MLG)**
- High-volume, low-ACV, content/community drives organic demand
- Signals: SEO opportunity large, community around topic, education is purchase trigger
- Metrics: CAC, payback period, organic vs. paid split, content attribution
- Examples: HubSpot, Canva, Webflow

**Hybrid motion** (most common in 2025):
- PLG for SMB and developer user acquisition
- SLG for enterprise land-and-expand
- MLG for brand and organic pipeline
- Transition signal: when >20% of PLG accounts have >10 seats, add SLG overlay

### Beachhead Strategy

The beachhead is the minimum wedge that (1) you can dominate and (2) gives you the right to expand.

**Beachhead criteria:**
- Small enough to win completely (you can reach all customers)
- Adjacent to a much larger market you want eventually
- Customers have acute pain (not chronic/tolerable)
- You can charge enough to learn from them

**Geoffrey Moore's bowling pin model**: Knock down the beachhead pin (vertical + use case + geography) → adjacent pins fall through credibility and product extension.

**2025 vertical SaaS beachhead playbook:**
- Target a 1,000-company vertical with $50K+ ACV potential
- Solve the workflow problem that's unique to that vertical
- Build the network effect within the vertical before expanding
- Use customer stories from the vertical to expand to adjacent verticals

### Pricing Strategy

**Value-based pricing framework** (SaaS):
1. Identify the value metric — what unit most closely tracks the value you deliver? (seats, API calls, events, revenue %)
2. Estimate customer ROI — 10x rule: price at 10% of the value delivered
3. Design tiers: Free/Freemium → Pro → Business → Enterprise
4. Package by persona, not by features

**Common pricing models and when to use:**
- Per seat: sales tool, communication, collaboration
- Usage-based: API, data, infrastructure — best for PLG as it scales with customer success
- Outcome-based: % of revenue/savings generated — premium positioning, hard to operationalize
- Flat/module: enterprise compliance tools, legal, HR
- Freemium: when viral loop exists and free users bring paid users

**Pricing anti-patterns:**
- Competing on price in a winner-take-all market (race to zero)
- Per-seat pricing for a tool that benefits from broad adoption (use team/org tiers)
- Not charging early customers (destroys your ability to discover WTP)

## 4. AI-Era Moat Building

### What Changed in the LLM Era

Pre-LLM moats: proprietary data, custom ML models, unique integrations
Post-LLM challenge: LLMs can replicate most information-processing workflows without unique data

**Moats that survived the LLM transition:**
- **Proprietary data** (still the strongest): data competitors can't access even with foundation models
- **Workflow lock-in**: switching costs from deep integration into business process
- **Network effects on data**: the more customers use you, the better your model gets (feedback loop)
- **Distribution**: existing customer relationships and trust
- **Brand**: the "trusted source" brand for a category

**Moats that didn't survive:**
- "Our ML model is better" — foundation models commoditized most ML inference
- "We have a feature competitors don't" — features are now copyable in weeks
- "Our data scientists built it" — most analytics workflows are GPT-copyable

### AI-Native Moat Patterns

**Data flywheel**: each customer interaction generates labeled data → improves model → attracts more customers
- Requirement: you have to capture the feedback signal (clicks, corrections, outcomes)
- Example: GitHub Copilot (code completions), Cursor (edit acceptance), Harvey (legal outcome data)

**Workflow moat**: so deeply embedded in the business process that switching requires organizational change
- Requirement: you own the record of truth (CRM, code repo, contract database)
- Example: Rippling (all HR data in one system), Linear (all engineering work in one system)

**Agent network effect**: multi-agent systems where agents trained on your platform outperform those trained elsewhere
- Earliest evidence: Salesforce Einstein (CRM data training) > generic agents for CRM tasks

## 5. Brand Strategy & Positioning

### Positioning Theory (Al Ries & Jack Trout + Marty Neumeier)

**Neumeier's brand definition**: "A brand is not a logo. A brand is a person's gut feeling about a product, service, or company."

**The positioning question**: "What is the one thing you want to own in the customer's mind?"

**Zag methodology** (Neumeier):
- When everyone zigs, zag — brand differentiation requires being noticeably different, not better
- Checklist: Is your brand (1) different? (2) compelling to your target audience? (3) authentic to what you actually do?

**Positioning statement template** (Geoffrey Moore):
> For [target customer] who [has this need], [Product] is a [market category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [primary competitor], [Product] [differentiating characteristic].

**Category design** (Play Bigger framework):
- The highest-leverage move: design a new category where you are automatically the leader
- Requirements: give the category a name + problem definition + solution definition
- Category designers capture 76% of the market value in their category (Play Bigger research)
- Examples: Salesforce ("CRM"), Slack ("team messaging"), Figma ("collaborative design")

### Brand Voice Architecture

**Voice dimensions:**
- Personality: professional ↔ casual, serious ↔ playful, expert ↔ accessible
- Tone: the adjustment of personality based on context (support = more serious than marketing)
- Language: the specific words you use (and avoid)

**Brand vocabulary exercise:**
- List 10 words that describe your brand
- List 10 words your brand would never use
- Test: does every piece of content feel like it came from the same person?

**Tone of voice for AI-era tech brands** (2025 patterns):
- Linear: brutally minimal, confident, technical precision
- Vercel: aspirational, developer-first, "ship it" energy
- Stripe: intelligent, precise, enterprise trust + developer approachability
- Loom: warm, human, empathetic — productivity tools benefit from human voice

## 6. Pitch Deck Architecture

### The Sequoia Pitch Structure (updated for 2025)

**10-slide core narrative:**

1. **Cover**: Company name + 6-word descriptor + contact
2. **Problem**: The customer's world as it is today — make them feel the pain
3. **Solution**: Your approach — show, don't describe. Live demo > screenshots > description.
4. **Why now**: What changed in the world that makes this the right moment? (regulatory, technological, behavioral)
5. **Market size**: TAM/SAM/SOM with bottom-up calculation. Never top-down only ("2% of $10B market")
6. **Product**: 3-5 key product differentiators with evidence (NPS scores, retention data, customer quotes)
7. **Business model**: Revenue mechanics, unit economics, pricing
8. **Traction**: The most compelling evidence you have. Revenue > growth > engagement > qualitative.
9. **Team**: Why you? What unique insight/access/capability does this team have?
10. **Ask**: The round size, use of funds, and the milestone that unlocks the next round

**Narrative rule**: Each slide should answer a question the investor is silently asking. If you can't articulate the question, cut the slide.

### The "Why Now" Slide

The highest-leverage slide for early-stage. Answer: what changed that makes this possible/necessary now?

**Categories of "why now":**
- Technology shift: new infrastructure enables what wasn't possible (LLMs, edge computing, biometrics)
- Regulatory change: new compliance requirement creates buying urgency
- Market event: economic shift changes buyer behavior or priorities
- Cultural shift: customer behavior changed (remote work, creator economy, Gen Z buying power)

**Anti-patterns:**
- "The market is large" — not a why now
- "We've been working on this for 3 years" — not a why now
- "We're passionate about the problem" — not a why now

### Metrics That Matter by Stage

| Stage | Primary Metric | Secondary Metrics |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-seed | Evidence of problem (user interviews) | Founder credentials, prototype quality |
| Seed | Early retention / engagement | Revenue (if any), growth rate, qualitative PMF |
| Series A | Revenue + growth rate (MoM) | CAC payback, NRR, gross margin |
| Series B | ARR + growth + NRR + unit economics | Sales efficiency, market expansion thesis |
| Series C+ | Path to profitability | Rule of 40, LTV/CAC, market share |

## 7. Product-Market Fit Diagnostics

### PMF Frameworks

**Sean Ellis PMF benchmark**: "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?" → >40% say "very disappointed" = PMF signal. Below 40% = keep iterating.

**Superhuman PMF engine** (Rahul Vohra):
- Segment "very disappointed" users by persona
- Interview them to understand what they love (the "very disappointed moment")
- Build more of what those users love
- Convert "somewhat disappointed" users with the same narrative

**Retention-based PMF** (Brian Balfour):
- Draw your retention curve — does it flatten or approach zero?
- Flat retention curve (any level) = product has a natural fit with some audience
- Sloping to zero = no PMF regardless of what users say in surveys

**NRR as PMF signal** (SaaS):
- NRR >100%: you have product-market fit with existing customers (they expand)
- NRR <80%: churn is exceeding expansion; no durable PMF
- NRR 80-100%: partial PMF; existing customers stay but don't grow

### PMF vs. Go-To-Market Fit

Common confusion: teams declare "PMF" when they've achieved GTM fit (sales motion works) but not true PMF (product delivers on its core promise). Test:
- Remove all sales activity for 30 days — do customers still renew and refer?
- This tests whether you have product PMF or just GTM efficiency

## 8. Creative Strategy & Execution

### Brand Identity System

**Visual identity hierarchy:**
1. Logo (wordmark or symbol) — the anchor
2. Color palette (primary + secondary + semantic) — the mood
3. Typography (3 typefaces max: display, body, mono) — the voice in visual form
4. Iconography system — the grammar
5. Photography/illustration style — the world
6. Motion guidelines — the personality in time

**2025 startup brand aesthetic patterns:**
- Brutalist minimal (Linear, Raycast): monochrome, tight spacing, no decoration
- Neon gradient (consumer crypto, gaming): dark backgrounds, electric accent colors
- Friendly precision (Notion, Coda): rounded corners, warm grays, humanist typefaces
- Enterprise trust (Stripe, Brex): clean, professional, subtle color, financial confidence

**Brand system tools** (2025):
- Figma: primary brand design and component library
- Framer: website design with component system
- Lottie: motion assets that export to web/mobile
- Canva Teams: brand kit distribution for non-designers

### Landing Page Architecture

**The 10-second test**: Visitors should understand in 10 seconds (1) what it does, (2) who it's for, (3) why it's different.

**Above-the-fold formula:**
- Headline: the one-line value proposition (job-to-be-done language)
- Sub-headline: the mechanism (how it delivers the promise)
- CTA: specific action, not "Learn More"
- Social proof: logos or stats that reduce anxiety

**Page structure** (for B2B SaaS):
1. Hero: problem + solution + CTA
2. Social proof: logos / testimonials
3. Product demo: animated GIF or video (80% watch rate > screenshots)
4. Feature benefits: 3 core differentiators (benefit first, feature second)
5. Use cases: 3 specific scenarios showing product in context
6. Pricing (optional above the fold): if PLG, show pricing to filter leads
7. Testimonials: specific outcomes with metrics
8. CTA repeat: lower friction offer (free trial vs. contact sales)

## 9. Expert Heuristics

**On strategy:**
- The goal is not to be better — it's to be different in a way that matters to the right customer. Better is temporary; different is strategic.
- The beachhead has to be small enough to dominate, not just enter. "Small enough to dominate" means you can reach every potential customer with your current resources.
- Counter-positioning is the most reliable startup strategy: find a business model the incumbent can't copy without cannibalizing themselves.

**On PMF:**
- Don't over-engineer the search for PMF. Talk to 50 customers, find the 20 who are "very disappointed," and build more of what they love.
- The leading indicator of PMF isn't revenue — it's the percent of users who would be very disappointed if you shut down. Revenue can be bought; that sentiment cannot.
- PMF is not a destination. It's a balance you reach and then lose when the market shifts. Stay in the loop.

**On brand:**
- Brand is the story customers tell themselves about why they chose you over someone else. Your job is to give them a story worth telling.
- The most underused brand strategy is "smallest defensible definition" — define your category so narrowly that you are obviously the leader.
- In AI-era products: technical capability is table stakes. Brand is the reason someone chooses you among 10 equally capable options.

**On fundraising narrative:**
- Investors fund the future, not the present. The pitch's job is to make the future feel inevitable.
- The "why now" slide is the investment thesis. The rest is evidence that you're the right team to capture it.
- VCs have seen 1,000 decks. The only ones they remember are the ones with a contrarian insight that turned out to be right.
