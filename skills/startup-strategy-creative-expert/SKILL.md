## 1. Product Strategy Frameworks

### Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD)

Customers do not buy products — they hire them to accomplish a "job." The job has three dimensions: functional (the practical task), emotional (how they want to feel), and social (how they want to be perceived).

**Operational application:**
1. Map the job: identify 10–20 discrete steps the customer takes to complete the job (first thought → final outcome)
2. Find unmet outcomes: within each step, identify 5–10 desired outcomes they cannot achieve well — these are innovation opportunities
3. Validate with switch interviews: ask users why they stopped using prior products — reveals the jobs that pull customers from incumbents
4. Build your roadmap around the steps where the job is most underserved

**2025 combination:** Link JTBD to your North Star Metric — the NSM should directly represent value delivered on the customer's core job (e.g., Slack's NSM is "messages sent per active user" because the job is "coordinate team communication without context-switching").

### Product-Market Fit (PMF) Measurement Stack

PMF is a state, not a milestone — you either have it, are approaching it, or are losing it.

| Signal | Method | Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Desire | Sean Ellis Test: "How disappointed if you could no longer use this?" | ≥40% say "Very disappointed" |
| Retention | Cohort retention curve | Curve flattens (doesn't go to zero) after week 4–8 |
| Advocacy | NPS | ≥40 B2C, ≥30 B2B |
| Organic pull | Word-of-mouth as % of new users | >30% organic |
| Engagement | DAU/MAU ratio | >25% SaaS, >50% consumer |

**Sean Ellis 40% Rule:** Companies above the 40% threshold grow 3–5x faster and retain significantly better (Amplitude research).

**"Hair on Fire" Problem:** Validate by asking: "What are you currently doing to solve this?" If the answer is "nothing," pain is not acute enough. If they describe duct-tape solutions, you have a hair-on-fire problem worth solving.

### Blue Ocean Strategy

**The Four Actions Framework (Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create):**
- **Eliminate:** Which factors the industry takes for granted should be eliminated?
- **Reduce:** Which factors should be reduced well below the industry standard?
- **Raise:** Which factors should be raised well above the industry standard?
- **Create:** Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered?

**2025 application:** In AI-era markets, blue ocean thinking means creating categories like "autonomous finance operations" rather than competing as "yet another accounting SaaS."

### Crossing the Chasm

The chasm lies between Early Adopters (visionaries) and the Early Majority (pragmatists). They use different references, respond to different proof, and buy differently.

**Crossing strategy:**
1. Choose a beachhead segment — a narrow, addressable niche within the early majority
2. Dominate it completely (80%+ market share is the goal)
3. Establish references and case studies within that niche
4. Expand to adjacent segments using the bowling pin model

## 2. Go-to-Market Strategy

### PLG vs. SLG vs. CLG

**Product-Led Growth (PLG):** Product itself is primary acquisition, activation, and expansion mechanism. Works when: ACV <$10K, self-serve decision-making, product value demonstrable in one session, viral coefficient >1 achievable through usage.

**Product-Led Sales (PLS) — The 2025 Hybrid:** PLG to land, SLG to expand. Expansion triggers: multiple power users from same domain, API usage spike, reaching free tier limits. Notion, Figma, Slack, and Datadog all operate this model (McKinsey research validates this is the dominant emerging pattern).

**Sales-Led Growth (SLG):** Required when: ACV >$25K, long sales cycles, multi-stakeholder buying committees, enterprise compliance requirements.

**Community-Led Growth (CLG):** Companies with strong communities grow revenue 2.1x faster than those without; every $1 invested in community returns $6.40 in value. Developer-tool startups (Stripe, Twilio, Vercel) use CLG as a primary moat. Takes 6–18 months to build — long-term play, not a quick lever.

### Network Effects Taxonomy

| Type | Mechanism | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Direct (same-side) | Value increases as more same-type users join | WhatsApp, Slack |
| Indirect (cross-side) | Value increases as complementary groups join | Uber, App Store |
| Data network effects | Product improves as more users generate data | Google, Waze, LinkedIn |
| Platform/marketplace | Each side's participation makes the other more valuable | Airbnb, Shopify |

### Virality Mechanics
K-factor = (invitations sent per user) × (conversion rate of invitations)
- K > 1: Exponential (viral loop)
- K < 1: Viral boost without viral loop

Sustainable viral loops are baked into the product workflow: Calendly (every shared calendar link is a touchpoint), DocuSign (every signature request recruits the recipient), Loom (every shared video is a brand ad).

## 3. Competitive Strategy

### Competitive Moats (Stack-Ranked by Durability for 2025)

1. **Proprietary Data Moat:** More than half of VCs cite proprietary data as the strongest durable advantage for AI startups. Data that improves your model over time — and that competitors cannot access — creates a compounding advantage.

2. **Network Effects:** Each user makes the product more valuable. Compound with data moats (every user improves the AI for all users) for maximum defensibility.

3. **Switching Costs:** Integration depth, workflow dependency, data portability lock-in. Jira, Salesforce, and Workday are protected by years of configuration and integrated workflows.

4. **Economies of Scale:** In AI, inference costs fall with model efficiency — companies with massive scale train better models more cheaply.

5. **Brand:** In AI, brand trust is functional, not just emotional — users rely on the product's judgment.

6. **Counter-Positioning:** A business model incumbents cannot adopt without cannibalizing their existing business. Netflix vs. Blockbuster.

7. **Cornered Resource:** Exclusive access to talent, regulatory license, patent, or partnership. FDA clearances, FedRAMP authorization, key engineering clusters.

**The AI-era moat insight:** Network effects are stronger with data flywheels; switching costs increase when AI tailors experience over time; brand matters more when users rely on the product to make decisions.

## 4. Brand Strategy for Tech Startups

### Brand Archetypes for Tech

| Archetype | Identity | Examples | Tone |
|---|---|---|---|
| The Creator | Build something meaningful | Figma, Adobe, Notion | Expressive, imaginative, empowering |
| The Hero | Overcome the odds | Salesforce, Shopify | Bold, confident, action-oriented |
| The Sage | Knowledge and wisdom | Google, IBM, HubSpot | Authoritative, clear, insightful |
| The Explorer | Freedom and discovery | Stripe, Vercel, Linear | Adventurous, independent, curious |
| The Ruler | Order and control | Oracle, SAP, AWS | Commanding, precise, established |

### Positioning Statement Formula
```
For [ICP], [Brand Name] is the [category] that [key benefit]
because [reason to believe].
```

### Naming Principles
- 1–2 syllables optimal (Stripe, Slack, Figma, Linear)
- .com remains valuable; .io and .ai are accepted in tech
- Avoid product-specific names (limits pivots)
- Coined words (Twilio, Zapier, Vercel) create trademark-ability with zero prior associations

### Category Creation vs. Category Entry

**Create when:** The problem is not yet named, no analyst coverage exists, prospects describe pain in disparate ways.

**Enter when:** Buyers have budget allocated, clear RFP criteria exist, you can win on differentiation within the existing frame.

**The category king captures 76% of total market economics.** Category creation requires 2–3x more investment in content, thought leadership, events, analyst relations, and takes 3–5 years to materialize.

**Category creation playbook:**
1. Name the problem first (before naming your product)
2. Publish the point of view: manifesto, book, report, conference
3. Build analyst and media relationships (Gartner, Forrester, G2 recognition)
4. Own the events (the category king hosts the conference)
5. Create the community (email lists, certifications, Slack communities)

## 5. AI-Era Startup Strategy (2025)

### Vertical AI vs. Horizontal Platforms

**Vertical AI defensibility advantages:**
1. **Proprietary domain data:** Pharma trial data, legal case files, financial audit records — sources others cannot access
2. **Deep workflow integration:** Embedded in specialized workflows, increasing switching costs
3. **Domain expertise:** Requires decades of institutional knowledge — LLM providers cannot invest this across every vertical
4. **Regulatory complexity:** Certifications, audit trails, compliance frameworks

**Market evidence:** Vertical AI solutions captured $3.5B in 2025 investment (nearly 3x the $1.2B in 2024). Leading vertical AI companies achieve 80% of traditional SaaS contract values with 400% YoY growth and 65% gross margins.

### Wrapper Startup Risk — The Test

Apply all criteria:
- Processing only publicly available information? → HIGH wrapper risk
- Can the model provider replicate your core feature in one API update? → HIGH wrapper risk
- Does your product improve as users use it (data flywheel)? → Defensible signal
- Do users configure or integrate the product deeply into their workflow? → Defensible signal
- Does your vertical require regulatory certification or compliance infrastructure? → Strong barrier

### Building on Foundation Models Safely
1. **Multi-model architecture:** Never depend on a single provider
2. **Fine-tune on proprietary data:** Even 10,000 domain-specific examples meaningfully differentiate outputs
3. **Own the workflow, not just the output:** Become the system of record
4. **Accumulate proprietary feedback loops:** User corrections, HITL approvals, domain expert annotations

### AI Defensibility Framework (Strongest → Weakest)
1. Proprietary training data that compounds over time
2. Deep workflow integration with high switching costs
3. Domain-expert team that competitors cannot hire
4. Regulatory certifications and government contracts
5. Model fine-tuning on exclusive datasets
6. Brand and trust in regulated industries (healthcare, finance, legal)

## 6. Narrative & Storytelling

### The Investor Narrative Arc

1. **The Problem (30 sec):** Crisp, visceral, with a specific person in a specific moment
2. **The Market (30 sec):** Bottom-up TAM from unit economics: "3M mid-market CFOs × $24K ACV = $72B addressable"
3. **The Solution (60 sec):** Show the outcome first: "Finance teams using [product] close in 2 days instead of 10"
4. **Why Now (20 sec):** The enabling condition — AI inference costs dropping 10x/year, regulation changing, platform shift
5. **Traction (60 sec):** ARR, growth rate, logos, NPS, retention — show the PMF signal
6. **Team (20 sec):** Unfair advantages — domain expertise, prior exits, unique networks
7. **The Ask (10 sec):** Clear, specific, use-of-funds breakdown

### Thought Leadership Strategy
- CEO-led content on LinkedIn is the highest-leverage thought leadership vehicle (CEOs with largest LinkedIn audiences attract highest investment levels)
- **Scalable Content Framework:** expertise → frameworks → case studies → opinion → community engagement
- **Content-led growth flywheel:** original research → media pickup → organic search → email list → pipeline

### Storytelling for Technical Products (Demo Design Hierarchy)
1. Lead with the before (the pain, the status quo)
2. Introduce the inciting incident (why now, why this problem matters)
3. Show the transformation (product demo — 60 seconds max for core value)
4. Reveal the after (business outcome, metric, emotional relief)
5. Proof (case study, customer quote, data)

## 7. Innovation Frameworks

### First Principles Thinking

**The method:**
1. State the problem as currently understood
2. Challenge every assumption: "Why does it have to work this way?"
3. Identify physics/economics constraints (real limits)
4. Identify social/convention constraints (these are opportunities)
5. Reconstruct from only what is physically/mathematically true

**Musk's canonical example:** Battery pack = cobalt, nickel, aluminum, steel, polymers, a can. Buy those on spot market: $80/kWh. This led to Tesla's battery cost advantage.

### SCAMPER (Fast Lateral Ideation)
- **Substitute:** What elements can be replaced? (Airbnb substituted hotels with spare bedrooms)
- **Combine:** What can be merged? (iPhone combined phone + iPod + browser)
- **Adapt:** Borrowed from another context? (Tinder adapted "hot or not" mechanic)
- **Modify/Magnify:** Exaggerated or emphasized? (Slack magnified presence awareness)
- **Put to other uses:** Alternate applications? (AWS repurposed internal infrastructure)
- **Eliminate:** What can be removed? (Google eliminated everything except the search box)
- **Reverse/Rearrange:** Inverted process? (Netflix reversed Blockbuster's model)

### Build-Measure-Learn

**Validated learning hierarchy:**
1. Smoke tests (landing page, fake door) — validate demand before building
2. Concierge MVP — do the job manually before automating
3. Wizard of Oz MVP — simulate automation with human behind the scenes
4. Functional MVP — build only the core job, nothing else

**2025 implementation:** Use feature flags (LaunchDarkly, Statsig, Unleash) to run A/B tests on shipped code — collapses the loop from weeks to hours.

### Amazon's Working Backwards
Write a Press Release + FAQ before any code:
- **Press Release:** Announces the finished product to the customer, in customer language — if you can't write it, you don't understand what you're building
- **FAQ:** Anticipates every customer, internal, and investor question — forces resolution of ambiguity before engineering begins

## 8. Organizational Strategy

### North Star Metric

A single metric that captures core value delivered to users.

| Company | NSM | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Airbnb | Nights booked | Captures value for both hosts and guests |
| Spotify | Time spent listening | Measures engagement and content value |
| Slack | Messages sent per DAU | Reflects team communication value |
| HubSpot | # customers using 3+ tools | Measures platform stickiness |
| Duolingo | DAU/MAU ratio | Captures habit formation |

### OKR Anti-Patterns to Avoid
- Setting KPIs as Key Results (outputs, not outcomes)
- More than 3 objectives, more than 5 KRs each
- Making OKRs the performance review system (kills ambition — they should be stretch goals)
- Bottom-up OKRs without top-down alignment

**Critical insight:** "OKRs don't create alignment — they amplify whatever alignment already exists, including misalignment." The pre-work for OKRs is strategic alignment on company direction.

### Two-Pizza Teams (Amazon)
The real innovation was not team size but **single-threaded ownership** — one team owns one service with no shared dependencies. This + microservices architecture enabled Amazon's velocity.

**Spotify Squad Model warning:** Spotify itself never fully implemented this model. The 2014 diagrams were internal experiments, not blueprints. Cargo-culting creates coordination overhead without the autonomy benefits.

## 9. Market Timing

### Timing Framework
Bill Gurley and Mike Maples research: being too early is as fatal as too late, but for different reasons:
- **Too early:** Infrastructure doesn't exist, customer education consumes capital
- **Too late:** Market is locked, differentiation is marginal
- **Right moment:** The enabling condition just changed

### Macro Trend Identification
1. Find a technology whose cost is dropping 10x/year or more
2. Find a regulation creating new compliance requirements
3. Find a behavior shift driven by generational change or global event
4. Find an incumbent with structural inability to respond (innovator's dilemma)
5. Intersection of two or more = timing signal

### Regulatory Tailwinds (2025)
- EU AI Act implementation → AI governance tools
- SEC cybersecurity disclosure rules → security posture management
- Healthcare interoperability (TEFCA) → healthcare data platforms
- Open banking → embedded finance

## 10. Creative Problem Solving

### Design Thinking (5 Stages)
1. **Empathize:** Deep user research — observation, interviews, immersion in context
2. **Define:** Synthesize into "How might we...?" problem statement
3. **Ideate:** Divergent brainstorming — quantity before quality, defer judgment
4. **Prototype:** Rough, cheap, fast representations
5. **Test:** Expose to real users, observe, iterate

**Google Ventures Sprint (5-day compression):** Monday (map), Tuesday (sketch), Wednesday (decide), Thursday (prototype), Friday (test with 5 users).

### Brainstorming Facilitation Best Practices
1. Separate diverge from converge (never evaluate during ideation)
2. Crazy 8s: fold paper into 8 panels, sketch 8 ideas in 8 minutes
3. "How Might We" reframing: "How might we make onboarding feel like a game?"
4. Silent brainstorming first (reduces anchoring bias from loudest voice)
5. Analogical thinking: "What would Disney do?" / "How would Amazon solve this?"

### Prototyping Culture Principles
- Prototype in the lowest-fidelity medium that generates real signal
- Separate "is this the right idea?" from "are we building it right?"
- Build a "prototype culture" by celebrating learnings from failures — the fastest-learning team wins
