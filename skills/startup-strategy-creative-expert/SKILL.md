# Startup Strategy + Creative Expert

## Core Strategic Frameworks

### Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) Framework
JTBD is the foundational lens for product strategy. Customers "hire" products to make progress in their lives — understanding the functional, emotional, and social dimensions of that job defines your real competition.

**Clayton Christensen's canonical form:**
> "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]."

**Two schools:**
- **Milkshake School** (Christensen): Circumstances-based, observational, functional job focus
- **Switch/Narrative School** (Bob Moesta, Chris Spiek): Four forces of progress: push of current situation, pull of new solution, anxiety of new solution, attachment to old solution

**Practical application for startups:**
1. Conduct "switch interviews" — find customers who recently switched to your solution or a competitor
2. Map the timeline of purchase: first thought → passive looking → active looking → deciding event → consuming
3. Identify the "struggling moment" — the precise trigger that causes a switch
4. Use JTBD to size the market correctly: you compete against all solutions hired for the job, including doing nothing

### Blue Ocean Strategy (Kim & Mauborgne)
Move from red oceans (fierce competition in existing market) to blue oceans (uncontested market space) by reconstructing market boundaries.

**Strategy Canvas:** Plot your industry on axes of key competing factors. Your value curve vs. competitors reveals differentiation opportunities.

**ERRC Framework:**
- **Eliminate** factors the industry competes on that deliver little value
- **Reduce** factors below industry standard
- **Raise** factors above industry standard
- **Create** factors the industry has never offered

**Six paths to blue ocean:**
1. Look across alternative industries (not just competitors)
2. Look across strategic groups within industries
3. Look across buyer chain (influencer → purchaser → user)
4. Look across complementary offerings
5. Look across functional-emotional orientation
6. Look across time (megatrends)

### Crossing the Chasm (Geoffrey Moore)
The Technology Adoption Life Cycle has a structural gap — the "chasm" — between early adopters (visionaries) and early majority (pragmatists).

**Key insight:** Pragmatists buy from other pragmatists. Visionary references don't transfer.

**Bowling pin strategy:**
1. Identify a beachhead segment — a niche where you can dominate completely
2. Win that segment with a complete solution (whole product)
3. Use that reference base to bowl into adjacent segments
4. Each segment win is a pin that tips others

**Whole Product Model:** Your core product + what else must be in place for the customer to get the complete solution. Fast followers often beat pioneers by offering a more complete whole product.

**Target customer for chasm crossing:**
- Pragmatist
- Has a genuine burning problem
- Has budget authority
- Can give you a referenceable win

## Product-Market Fit (PMF) Methodology

### Sean Ellis Test (40% Rule)
Ask: "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?"
- Very disappointed
- Somewhat disappointed
- Not disappointed

**Threshold:** ≥40% "very disappointed" = PMF signal. Below 40% = do not scale.

**Context:** Slack hit 51% early. Airbnb hit 48%. Most Series A companies are at 30-38% and are optimizing.

### Superhuman Methodology (Rahul Vohra)
1. Run Ellis survey segmented by user cohort
2. Identify the segment most likely to say "very disappointed" (your PMF segment)
3. Ask "very disappointed" users what they love most → core value
4. Ask "somewhat disappointed" users what would make them love it → improvement signal
5. Double down on core value; address top improvement for the likely-to-love segment

### Retention-Based PMF
Ultimately PMF shows in retention curves:
- Consumer: 25-40% D30 retention (varies by category)
- SaaS: 85%+ annual retention, negative churn at scale
- Marketplace: GMV growth + supplier retention

**Retention curve flattening:** If your retention curve flattens at any positive level, you have a retention floor — PMF signal. If it curves toward zero, you do not.

### Demand-Side vs. Supply-Side PMF
- **Demand-side**: Users actively seek your solution (organic search, word-of-mouth)
- **Supply-side**: You can efficiently acquire users at profitable unit economics

Both must be true for scalable PMF. Supply-side only = growth by force. Demand-side only = niche business.

## Go-to-Market Strategy

### GTM Motion Types
**Product-Led Growth (PLG):**
- Product is the primary driver of acquisition, conversion, and expansion
- Freemium or free trial is the acquisition mechanism
- PQL (Product Qualified Lead) replaces MQL
- North Star: time-to-value, activation rate, product engagement score
- Best for: horizontal tools, developer tools, productivity software
- Examples: Figma, Notion, Linear, Vercel

**Sales-Led Growth (SLG):**
- Human-driven enterprise sales motion
- ICP definition, outbound sequencing, demo → POC → contract
- North Star: pipeline coverage (3-4x), win rate, ASP, cycle time
- Best for: complex enterprise, high-touch integration, regulated industries
- Examples: Salesforce, Workday, ServiceNow

**Community-Led Growth (CLG):**
- Community is the distribution moat
- OSS + community → SaaS conversion (HashiCorp model)
- Developer evangelism, content flywheel, conference strategy
- Examples: Hashicorp, Grafana Labs, Hugging Face, Discord

**Partner-Led / Channel:**
- Resellers, MSPs, ISVs, technology alliances
- Requires partner portal, co-sell motions, deal registration
- CAC leverage but margin sharing

**Product-Led Sales (PLS) — Hybrid:**
- PLG for top-of-funnel + SLG for enterprise conversion
- Identify PQLs → route to sales for land-and-expand
- OpenView's thesis: PLS companies grow 2x faster with 20% better NDR
- Threshold signal for PLS: self-serve generates ≥30% of ARR organically

### ICP (Ideal Customer Profile) Definition
Beyond firmographics (size, industry, geography), define:
- **Technographics**: stack compatibility, tech maturity
- **Behavioral**: usage patterns, content consumption, previous solutions tried
- **Psychographic**: risk tolerance, build-vs-buy orientation, innovation adoption

**ICP scoring model:** Weight each attribute, score prospects 0-100, define A/B/C tiers.

**Negative ICP**: Define who you explicitly don't sell to (wrong size, wrong stage, wrong use case) — prevents churn from misfit customers.

### GTM Playbook Components
1. **Positioning statement**: For [target customer] who [has this need], [product] is a [category] that [unique benefit]. Unlike [competitor], we [differentiator].
2. **Messaging hierarchy**: Category claim → differentiating claim → proof points
3. **Channel strategy**: Paid (SEM/social), organic (SEO/content), outbound (SDR/AE), PLG, partnerships
4. **Sales motion**: SDR/AE ratio, quota design, comp plan, territories
5. **Pricing and packaging**: Metric selection, tier architecture, expansion triggers

### Pricing Strategy
**Value-based pricing is the goal.** Cost-plus and competitor-parity are traps.

**SaaS pricing metrics by category:**
- Users/seats: HubSpot, Slack, Figma
- Usage/consumption: AWS, Stripe, Twilio
- Outcomes/results: performance marketing platforms
- Records/entities: Salesforce contacts, HubSpot contacts

**Pricing psychology:**
- Anchor with highest tier first (decoy effect)
- Charm pricing ($97 vs $100) has diminishing returns in B2B
- Annual plans with 15-20% discount improve cash flow and reduce churn by 30%+
- Price increases: benchmark 7-10% annually; announce with value delivery

**AI product pricing (2025):**
- Token-based consumption: aligns cost structure but creates unpredictability
- Outcome-based: charge per task completed (Devin, Harvey AI model)
- Seat + usage hybrid: growing preference (Cursor, GitHub Copilot model)

## Network Effects and Growth Loops

### Network Effects Taxonomy (NFX Framework)
**16 types of network effects** (James Currier, NFX):

**Direct network effects:**
1. Physical (landline telephone)
2. Protocol (TCP/IP, Ethereum)
3. Personal utility (WhatsApp, iMessage)
4. Personal (Facebook social graph)
5. Market network (LinkedIn, Alignable)

**Indirect network effects:**
6. 2-sided marketplace (Airbnb, Uber)
7. Platform (iOS App Store, Shopify)
8. Asymptotic marketplace (Craigslist, eBay)

**Data network effects:**
9. Data (Waze, Duolingo, Google Maps)
10. Tech performance (speech recognition, ad algorithms)
11. Language/cultural (emojis, internet slang)

**Social network effects:**
12. Bandwagon (Zoom during COVID)
13. Tribal (Peloton, CrossFit, Slack channels)
14. Hub-and-spoke (WeChat mini-programs)

**Value stacking:**
15. 2-sided platform + data NfX (Airbnb improving matching)
16. Platform + protocol (iOS + apps as protocol)

**NfX Defensibility Rank:** Direct > 2-sided marketplace > Platform > Data > Asymptotic

### Growth Loops vs. Funnels (Reforge Framework)
Funnels are linear and require constant re-filling. Loops are compounding.

**Loop formula:** Output of step N becomes input of step 1

**Types of growth loops:**
1. **Viral loop**: User → shares/invites → new user (Dropbox referral, Slack invite)
2. **Content loop**: User creates content → SEO/distribution → new user discovers → creates content
3. **Paid acquisition loop**: Revenue → paid ads → new revenue (requires LTV > CAC)
4. **Product loop**: Usage → data → better product → more usage → retention (Waze, Spotify Discover Weekly)
5. **Community loop**: Members → create value → attract new members → more value

**Loop diagnostic questions:**
- What does each user do that could bring in another user?
- What data does usage generate that improves the product?
- What content does the product create that Google/social can distribute?

**Compound growth math:** A 5% weekly loop vs. a 4% weekly loop compounds to a 12.6x performance difference over 52 weeks.

## Competitive Strategy and Moats

### Moat Taxonomy (Hamilton Helmer, 7 Powers)
1. **Scale economies**: Cost advantages that grow with volume (Netflix content, AWS infrastructure)
2. **Network economies**: Value increases with users (LinkedIn, Visa)
3. **Counter-positioning**: New business model incumbents can't copy without damaging existing business (Netflix vs. Blockbuster)
4. **Switching costs**: Cost of moving away from a product (Salesforce data + integrations, Oracle ERP)
5. **Branding**: Durable price premium from trust and identity (Apple, Rolex)
6. **Cornered resource**: Exclusive access to a valued asset (patents, data, talent)
7. **Process power**: Embedded operational advantages that can't be rapidly replicated (Toyota Production System, Amazon fulfillment)

### AI Startup Defensibility — The Wrapper Problem
AI wrappers (thin OpenAI/Anthropic API wrappers) are extremely vulnerable:
- OpenAI/Anthropic can launch competing features
- Competitors replicate features in weeks
- No switching costs, no data moat

**Building defensibility as an AI startup:**
1. **Proprietary data flywheel**: Usage generates training data that improves model that increases usage (Harvey AI legal data, Suki medical dictation data)
2. **Workflow integration depth**: Become a system of record, not just a surface (embedded in EHR, CRM, IDE)
3. **Human-in-the-loop network effects**: Expert feedback loops create unique labeled data (Scale AI, Labelbox)
4. **Fine-tuned vertical models**: Domain-specific models competitors can't easily replicate (Insilico Medicine for drug discovery)
5. **Multi-step automation lock-in**: Automate complex multi-step workflows — the more steps, the higher switching cost
6. **Community + brand in developer tools**: Cursor, Codeium, Warp's developer mindshare

**AI defensibility matrix (2025):**
- Weak: API wrapper with GPT-4 prompt + UI
- Medium: Vertical fine-tuned model + workflow integration
- Strong: Proprietary dataset + fine-tuned model + workflow depth + network effects
- Very Strong: Platform with developer ecosystem + proprietary data + distribution partnership

## OKRs, North Star Metrics, and Strategic Planning

### OKR Framework (Objectives and Key Results)
**Structure:** 3-5 objectives per level, 3-5 KRs per objective.

**Quality criteria for OKRs:**
- **Objectives**: Qualitative, inspirational, directional — not tasks
- **Key Results**: Measurable, verifiable, outcomes (not outputs), time-bound

**Two types of KRs:**
1. **Activity KRs** (anti-pattern): "Launch new feature by Q3" — outputs, not outcomes
2. **Metric KRs** (correct): "Increase weekly active users from 10K to 20K by Q3-end"

**OKR grading:** 0.7 is success (0.3-0.7 learning zone, 1.0 means not ambitious enough). Google grades 0.0-1.0.

**OKR cadence:**
- Annual company OKRs (strategic horizon)
- Quarterly team OKRs (aligned to annual)
- Weekly check-ins (confidence scores, blockers)
- Monthly reviews (scoring, learning capture)

**OKR anti-patterns to avoid:**
- Too many OKRs (≤5 objectives per level)
- KRs as task lists
- OKRs as performance reviews (separates accountability)
- No connection between company and team OKRs
- Setting-and-forgetting (no cadence)

### North Star Metric (NSM)
A single metric that best captures the core value your product delivers to customers and predicts long-term revenue growth.

**NSM selection criteria:**
1. Represents value delivered to customer
2. Predicts long-term retention
3. Measurable and actionable
4. Lagging indicator of health (not easily gamed)

**NSM examples:**
- Airbnb: Nights booked
- Facebook: Daily active users (7 friends in 10 days)
- Slack: Messages sent per active user
- Duolingo: Daily active learners (DAU)
- Spotify: Time spent listening
- Amplitude: Weekly learning users
- HubSpot: Weekly active teams using ≥3 hubs

**NSM decomposition (input metrics):**
Break NSM into 3-5 drivers (lever tree). Each team owns a lever. Growth = Sum(lever improvements).

**NSM failure modes:**
- Vanity metrics (total signups, page views)
- Revenue as NSM (lags value delivery, not predictive)
- Composite/unmeasurable metrics
- Gaming the metric (session inflation, manufactured activity)

## Brand Strategy and Creative

### Brand Architecture
**Monolithic**: Single master brand across all products (Apple, Google, Amazon)
- Pros: Halo effect, brand equity transfer
- Cons: One crisis damages all

**Endorsed**: Sub-brands endorsed by parent (Marriott Bonvoy, Courtyard by Marriott)
- Pros: Flexibility with credibility transfer
- Cons: Complexity, consumer confusion

**House of brands**: Standalone brands with no visible parent (P&G, Unilever, IAC)
- Pros: Isolation, targeting different segments
- Cons: High marketing cost, no synergy

**For startups:** Usually monolithic or simple endorsed makes sense. House of brands only at scale.

### Positioning Frameworks
**April Dunford's "Obviously Awesome" 10-step positioning process:**
1. Understand who you lose deals to
2. List your best customers
3. List the differentiated capabilities
4. Identify what capabilities make you better than alternatives
5. Find patterns in best customers
6. Find the value these customers get from your capabilities
7. Define the market categories you can win
8. Layer in relevant trends
9. Write your positioning
10. Test and validate

**Category creation vs. category competition:**
Creating a category is 10x harder but creates durable leadership. Requires:
- Evangelize the problem (not your solution)
- Commission the research ("State of X" reports)
- Build a community around the problem
- Get analysts to define the category (Gartner Magic Quadrant placement)
- Examples: Salesforce created "CRM", HubSpot created "Inbound Marketing", Gong created "Revenue Intelligence"

### Pitch Deck Structure (12-16 Slides)
**Seed/Series A winning structure:**
1. **Title slide**: Company name, tagline, date, contact
2. **Problem**: The pain, who has it, why it matters now (data + emotion)
3. **Solution**: What you built, demo or visual, why it's 10x better
4. **Product**: Screenshots/demo flow, key features, UX differentiation
5. **Market size**: TAM/SAM/SOM, bottom-up sizing, why now
6. **Business model**: How you make money, unit economics preview
7. **Traction**: ARR/MRR, growth rate, key customers, logos
8. **Go-to-market**: How you acquire customers, channels, CAC
9. **Competition**: Honest competitive landscape, your differentiation
10. **Team**: Why you? Domain expertise, previous wins, key hires
11. **Financials**: 3-year projection, key assumptions, path to profitability
12. **Ask**: Amount raising, use of funds, milestones this round buys
13. **Appendix**: Full unit economics, technical architecture, cohort data

**Deck design principles:**
- One idea per slide
- Data trumps adjectives ("3x faster" not "blazing fast")
- Tell a narrative arc: Problem-Solution-Market-Product-Traction-Team
- Lead with traction — if you have it, show it early
- Use Sequoia's "Why Now?" frame — macro tailwinds that make this the moment

### Storytelling and Narrative
**Andy Raskin's "Greatest Sales Deck":**
1. Name the enemy (old way, broken paradigm)
2. Answer "Why now?" (shift in the world)
3. Show the promised land (new way, before/after)
4. Position as the magic features (capabilities)
5. Present proof (traction, testimonials)

**Founder story arc (Y Combinator model):**
- Personal origin story tied to the problem
- "Hair on fire" moment of experiencing the problem
- Failed attempts with existing solutions
- Eureka insight leading to the solution
- Traction proving others share the pain

## Startup Strategy: Sequencing and Timing

### Stage-Specific Strategy
**Pre-PMF (seed):** Focus exclusively on PMF discovery. No hiring for scale, no GTM investment beyond learning. Run experiments, talk to customers, iterate fast.

**Early PMF (Series A):** Found a beachhead segment. Prove you can scale CAC in one channel. Build repeatable sales process. Hire specialists.

**Scaling (Series B+):** Proven GTM playbook. Layer on new channels, geographies, segments. Build organizational infrastructure. Shift from founders selling to professional GTM team.

### Strategic Choices Framework
**The Innovator's Dilemma application:** Incumbents consistently lose to entrants who start with worse products in low-margin segments and improve upmarket. As a startup:
- Attack from below on metrics incumbents ignore (performance, simplicity, cost)
- Target over-served customers who don't want all incumbent features
- Or target non-consumers (new markets)

**Jobs-to-be-Done + Blue Ocean synthesis:**
1. Find underserved jobs (JTBD)
2. Create new value curve to serve them (Blue Ocean ERRC)
3. Use crossing the chasm to dominate a beachhead
4. Build network effects or switching costs before scaling

### Key Metrics by Stage
| Stage | Revenue | Focus Metric | Key Question |
|-------|---------|-------------|--------------|
| Pre-Seed | $0 | Qualitative PMF | Does the problem exist? |
| Seed | $0-500K ARR | Ellis Test ≥40% | Is there a valuable job? |
| Series A | $1-5M ARR | NRR ≥100%, CAC payback <18mo | Can we scale the channel? |
| Series B | $5-30M ARR | Rule of 40, ARR growth 3x YoY | Is GTM repeatable? |
| Series C+ | $30M+ ARR | Gross margin expansion | Path to profitability? |

## AI-Native Strategy (2025-2026)

### The AI Strategy Stack
**Layer 1 - Intelligence layer**: Which models, fine-tuning strategy, evaluation rigor
**Layer 2 - Workflow layer**: Where AI augments or automates workflows
**Layer 3 - Data layer**: What proprietary data you collect, label, and use
**Layer 4 - Distribution layer**: How you reach customers at lower CAC than incumbents

**The "3D" AI startup framework:**
- **Data advantage**: Unique data competitors can't get
- **Domain depth**: Vertical expertise that shapes AI output quality
- **Distribution moat**: Owned channel or embedded partnership

### Vertical AI vs. Horizontal AI
**Vertical AI (strong thesis for startups):**
- Domain-specific training data
- Deep workflow integration
- Expert user feedback loops
- Examples: Harvey (legal), Suki (medical), Abridge (clinical documentation), Glean (enterprise search)
- Defensibility: Data + workflow = high switching cost

**Horizontal AI (hard for startups):**
- Competing on foundational model capabilities
- Hard to match foundation model providers (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google)
- Exception: specific capability unlocks (Cursor in IDE coding, ElevenLabs in voice)

### The Agentic GTM Shift (2025)
Traditional GTM assumes humans in the buying journey. AI agents are increasingly in the discovery, evaluation, and buying path:
- **AI-powered outbound**: Agents composing personalized sequences
- **AI-driven discovery**: Buyers use Claude/ChatGPT to evaluate solutions
- **Agent-to-agent transactions**: Future where AI agents purchase SaaS for humans

**Strategic implication**: Build structured data about your product for AI consumption. Ensure your product appears in AI model training data, documentation, and developer communities. Optimize for "AI SEO" — the way models describe your category.

## Creative Strategy for Startups

### Brand Voice Development
**Voice vs. Tone:** Voice is consistent (who you are), tone varies by context (how you speak in different situations).

**Voice spectrum exercise:**
- Plot on axes: formal ↔ casual, serious ↔ playful, corporate ↔ human, technical ↔ accessible
- Define your position on each axis
- Document with "we are X but not Y" statements
- Example: "We are confident but not arrogant. We are technical but not jargon-heavy. We are friendly but not informal."

**Brand voice attributes framework:**
- Identify 3-4 core brand traits
- For each trait: define it, show it in practice, show what it's not
- Document voice-kill phrases (words/phrases to avoid)

### Content Strategy
**Content types by funnel stage:**
- **TOFU**: SEO blog posts, YouTube tutorials, social thought leadership, podcast appearances, "State of X" reports
- **MOFU**: Case studies, comparison pages, webinars, templates/tools, ROI calculators
- **BOFU**: Pricing pages, demos, free trials, security docs, compliance docs, customer references

**Developer content strategy (for dev tools):**
- Documentation quality is table stakes — non-negotiable
- Tutorial videos (YouTube) compound for years
- Technical blog posts on problems you solved
- Open source contributions as distribution
- Discord/Slack community as support + feedback + virality

### Creative Differentiation
**Pattern interrupt principle:** In crowded markets, sameness kills. Do the opposite of industry conventions.
- Basecamp vs. every other PM tool (anti-meetings, anti-growth, radical simplicity)
- Monzo/Revolut vs. traditional banking (bright coral card, transparency-first)
- Notion vs. Confluence (design-forward, flexible structure)

**"10x vs. 10% better" heuristic:** Features beat competition by 10% get ignored. 10x improvements create category wins. Identify where your solution is 10x better and make that the lead.

**The jobs-based creative brief:**
- What job is the customer hiring this product for?
- What emotional jobs accompany the functional job?
- What social jobs (identity, status) are at play?
- How does our creative connect to those jobs?
