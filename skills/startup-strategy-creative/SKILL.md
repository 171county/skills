# Startup Strategy + Creative Expert

## 1. Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) Framework

The most actionable strategic framework for AI-era products: customers "hire" products to fulfill a job. The product wins when it does the job better than all alternatives.

### Job Map (Outcome-Driven Innovation, Ulwick)

Every job has 8 stages:
1. **Define** — establish goals, plan approach
2. **Locate** — gather information, inputs, materials
3. **Prepare** — set up, organize, configure
4. **Confirm** — validate readiness
5. **Execute** — carry out the core task
6. **Monitor** — check progress, ensure desired outcomes
7. **Modify** — adjust when needed
8. **Conclude** — finish up, wrap up, archive

**Application:** Map your product's features to each stage. Identify where customers have the most pain (underserved outcomes) — this is your roadmap.

### Forces of Progress (Jobs Switch Framework)

Customers switch to a new solution when:
- **Push**: frustration with existing solution reaches breaking point
- **Pull**: attraction to new solution's promise
- **Anxiety**: fear of switching
- **Habit**: comfort with the current way

To accelerate adoption: amplify push + pull, reduce anxiety + habit switching costs.

### JTBD Interview Template

```
Opening: "Tell me about the last time you did [job]..."
Timeline: "Walk me through what happened from the moment you decided 
           you needed to do [job]..."
Switching: "Had you tried other solutions before? What was that like?"
Struggle: "What was the hardest part? What frustrated you most?"
Outcome: "When you succeeded, how did you know it went well?"
Hiring: "What made you ultimately choose [product/approach]?"
```

**5 most important JTBD questions:**
1. When did you first think about doing this job?
2. What were you using before? Why wasn't it enough?
3. What would it look like if you failed?
4. Who else is affected when this job gets done badly?
5. If you could wave a magic wand and have it perfect, what would happen?

## 2. ICP Definition and Market Segmentation

### Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) Framework

An ICP is a detailed description of the company (B2B) or person (B2C) who gets the most value from your product and has the highest propensity to buy and retain.

**B2B ICP dimensions:**
```
Firmographic:
  Industry: e.g., Series B/C SaaS, healthcare providers, law firms
  Company size: employees, revenue range
  Geography: US-only, English-speaking, EMEA-ready
  Tech stack: AWS/GCP/Azure, Salesforce/HubSpot, GitHub-native
  
Behavioral:
  Existing tools they hate (pain signal)
  Annual budget cycle timing
  Decision-making speed (self-serve vs. committee)
  Current workaround complexity
  
Psychographic:
  Early adopter vs. pragmatist
  Technical buyer vs. business buyer
  Top-down vs. bottom-up decision making
  
Outcome signals (ICP fit score):
  Time-to-close < 30 days
  Deal size $5K–$20K ACV
  NPS > 70 at 90 days
  Expansion within 6 months
```

### Beachhead Market Strategy

Geoffrey Moore's Crossing the Chasm: pick the smallest viable market you can dominate before expanding.

**Beachhead selection criteria:**
- Whole product gap is closeable with current resources
- Your advantages are 10x better for this segment
- Word of mouth flows naturally to adjacent segments
- Revenue from beachhead funds expansion
- Competitors underserve this segment

**Beachhead → TAM expansion playbook:**
```
Beachhead: Healthcare billing AI for single-specialty practices
      ↓
Adjacent 1: Multi-specialty groups
      ↓  
Adjacent 2: Hospital systems
      ↓
Adjacent 3: International markets
      ↓
Platform: Full healthcare revenue cycle management
```

## 3. Positioning and Messaging

### April Dunford's Positioning Framework (Obviously Awesome)

Positioning answers: "What are you, for whom, versus what alternative?"

**5 components of a positioning statement:**
1. **Competitive alternatives** — what would customers do if you didn't exist?
2. **Unique attributes** — what features/capabilities do you have that alternatives lack?
3. **Value** — what value do your attributes enable?
4. **Target customers** — who cares deeply about that value?
5. **Market category** — what context makes your value obvious?

**Template:**
```
For [target customers] who [pain/desire],
[product name] is a [market category]
that [key benefit].
Unlike [competitive alternative],
[product] [key differentiation].
```

**Example (AI coding tool):**
```
For senior engineers at growth-stage startups who spend 40% of 
their time on boilerplate and context-switching,
CodeAgent Pro is an AI development partner
that understands your entire codebase and autonomously completes 
multi-file changes.
Unlike GitHub Copilot (which only suggests single lines), 
CodeAgent Pro executes complete feature implementations end-to-end.
```

### Message Hierarchy

```
Level 1 — Tagline (5–7 words): "Build software 10x faster"
Level 2 — Value prop (1 sentence): "The AI development partner that 
          understands your entire codebase"
Level 3 — Elevator pitch (3–4 sentences): core problem, solution, differentiation
Level 4 — Full story (2–3 paragraphs): journey, proof, call to action
```

## 4. Blue Ocean Strategy

Kim & Mauborgne's framework for making competition irrelevant by creating uncontested market space.

### Strategy Canvas and ERRC Grid

**Four Actions Framework:**
- **Eliminate**: which factors the industry competes on should be eliminated?
- **Reduce**: which factors should be reduced well below industry standard?
- **Raise**: which factors should be raised well above industry standard?
- **Create**: which factors should be created that the industry has never offered?

**Example — AI legal research tool vs. traditional legal research:**
```
ELIMINATE:  Per-minute billing, manual case review, paralegal supervision
REDUCE:     Setup time, training requirements, integration complexity
RAISE:      Citation accuracy, research speed, coverage breadth
CREATE:     Real-time regulatory change alerts, predictive case outcome scoring
```

### Non-Customer Analysis

Three tiers of non-customers:
1. **Soon-to-be**: on the edge of your market, minimal use
2. **Refusing**: consciously avoid the market
3. **Unexplored**: never considered your market category

The largest Blue Ocean opportunities live in tiers 2 and 3.

## 5. Go-to-Market Motions

### PLG (Product-Led Growth)

Product is the primary driver of acquisition, activation, retention, and expansion. Users discover value before talking to sales.

**PLG mechanics:**
- **Free tier or trial** that delivers meaningful value (not a "lite" version that's crippling)
- **Viral loop**: every user action expands product's reach (share, invite, export)
- **Activation metric**: the action that predicts retention (e.g., "user runs 3 queries in first week")
- **PQL (Product Qualified Lead)**: signal that a user is ready for sales expansion

**PLG funnel:**
```
Awareness → Signup → Activation → Engagement → Expansion → Advocacy
             ↓
          Freemium/Trial
             ↓
          PQL Signal → Sales
             ↓
          Paid Plan
```

**PLG activation formula:**
```
Activation Rate = Users who hit activation event / All signups
Target: >20% (strong), 10–20% (improving), <10% (product problem)
```

### SLG (Sales-Led Growth)

Traditional enterprise motion: sales rep-driven from outbound prospecting through close. Best when:
- ACV > $25K (sales can deliver ROI)
- Long enterprise buying cycles
- Complex security/compliance requirements
- Products requiring change management

**SLG velocity formula:**
```
Pipeline Velocity = (Opportunities × Win Rate × ACV) / Sales Cycle Length
Target: maximize velocity by improving each of the 3 numerators
```

### MLG (Marketing-Led Growth)

Content, SEO, events, and brand drive demand. Best for:
- Category creation (educating the market)
- Products with long consideration cycles
- Developer-focused tools (documentation as marketing)

**MLG channels by stage:**
```
0→$1M ARR: founder-led content, direct outreach, community
$1M→$5M: SEO, paid search, events, partnerships
$5M→$20M: full content engine, field marketing, ABM
$20M+: brand campaigns, analyst relations, partner ecosystem
```

### Hybrid GTM (2025 Standard)

Most AI-native companies use a PLG+SLG hybrid:
```
Bottoms-up: Individual users → PQL signal → Account executive upgrade
Top-down: Enterprise sales → Champions → Department rollout
```

## 6. Pricing Strategy

### Pricing Models for AI Products

| Model | Structure | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Seat-based** | Per user/month | Collaboration tools, uniform usage |
| **Usage-based** | Per API call, task, token | AI APIs, developer tools, metered value |
| **Outcome-based** | Per successful task | High-confidence AI, services replacement |
| **Hybrid** | Platform fee + usage | Enterprise AI with high baseline value |
| **Tiered SaaS** | Free/Pro/Team/Enterprise | Consumer → enterprise expansion |

**Usage-based pricing (most common for AI):**
```
Monthly Platform Fee: $199/month (includes 1000 tasks)
Overage: $0.10/task after 1000
Enterprise: Custom contract with committed minimums
```

**Value-based pricing process:**
1. Identify the value metric (what customers measure success by)
2. Calculate willingness-to-pay via customer interviews
3. Anchor 5x below the value they receive
4. Price 3x above your COGS floor

**Price anchoring in sales:**
- Lead with the highest tier first (enterprise)
- Show annual vs. monthly prominently (annual saves ~20%)
- Quantify ROI in proposal: "At your usage, you'll save $X/month"

## 7. Brand Strategy for AI Startups

### Brand Identity Architecture

**Brand foundations:**
- **Purpose**: why the company exists beyond profit
- **Vision**: the world you're creating
- **Mission**: what you do every day
- **Values**: 3–5 non-negotiable operating principles

**Brand personality dimensions (using Big Five model):**
```
Openness: experimental ↔ conventional
Conscientiousness: rigorous ↔ spontaneous
Extraversion: bold/expressive ↔ quiet/reserved
Agreeableness: warm/human ↔ direct/precise
Stability: confident/stable ↔ raw/urgent
```

**AI brand differentiation challenge (2025–2026):**
The market is flooded with AI brands using identical aesthetics (purple gradients, neural network visuals, "AI" prefix). Differentiation requires:
- Category creation over category joining
- Human outcomes over technology features
- Distinctive visual system (avoid the AI aesthetic tropes)
- Brand voice that takes a clear point of view

### Naming

**Framework for startup naming:**
1. **Functional**: describes what you do (DropBox, Zoom)
2. **Invented**: no prior meaning (Slack, Lyft)
3. **Metaphorical**: implies feeling or concept (Amazon, Apple)
4. **Founder's name**: personal brand (Harvey.ai)
5. **Acronym**: rare for new companies; hard to own

**Criteria checklist:**
- [ ] .com domain available (or .ai for AI-native)
- [ ] Trademark search clean in primary markets (USPTO, EU EUIPO)
- [ ] No offensive meanings in target languages
- [ ] Pronounceable by global audience
- [ ] Memorable in <5 seconds of exposure

## 8. Creative Direction and Visual Identity

### Design System for Startups

**Minimal viable brand kit:**
- 1 primary + 1–2 secondary colors
- 1 primary typeface + 1 supporting typeface
- Logo (full + mark only)
- Icon set (24–36 icons covering core use cases)
- Photography/illustration style guide

**Color strategy:**
```
Dominant (60%): Background, large areas — usually neutral
Secondary (30%): UI chrome, cards, secondary buttons
Accent (10%): CTAs, highlights, brand moments
```

**Typography hierarchy:**
```
Display (Hero): 48–72px — 1 statement per page
H1: 32–40px — Section headers
H2: 24–28px — Sub-section
Body: 14–16px — Regular reading
Caption: 11–12px — Labels, metadata
Monospace: Code, data
```

### Pitch Deck Design Principles

10 slides that work (based on patterns from successful Sequoia/a16z decks):
1. **Problem** — make them feel the pain (story, not bullet points)
2. **Solution** — demo > slides
3. **Market size** — bottoms-up is more credible than TAM/SAM/SOM charts
4. **Product** — 2–3 screenshots showing key magic
5. **Traction** — graph showing inflection; key metrics in one view
6. **Business model** — pricing, unit economics, path to gross margin
7. **Competition** — differentiation matrix (your strengths vs. alternatives)
8. **Team** — why this team? what credibility signals matter?
9. **Go-to-Market** — how will you reach $X ARR?
10. **The Ask** — how much, what it funds, milestones it achieves

## 9. Growth Loops

Growth loops are self-reinforcing cycles where each iteration of the loop brings in more inputs.

### Types of Growth Loops

**Viral loop:**
```
User creates value → Invites others → Others join → Create more value → Loop
```

**Content loop:**
```
Create SEO content → Rank → Traffic → Signups → User-generated content → More SEO
```

**Data network effect loop:**
```
Users generate data → Model improves → Better product → More users
```

**Marketplace loop:**
```
More supply → Better buyer experience → More buyers → More supply
```

### Implementing Growth Loops

**Viral coefficient (K-factor):**
```
K = (Invites sent per user) × (Conversion rate of invites)
K > 1.0 = viral growth (each user brings >1 new user)
K = 0.5 = linear growth amplifier (valuable but not self-sustaining)
```

**Activation → referral flywheel:**
```python
# Optimal timing for referral invitation
# Don't ask for referrals on day 1 — ask after "aha moment"

AHA_MOMENT_ACTIONS = ["completed_first_report", "shared_dashboard", "set_up_integration"]

def should_prompt_referral(user_events: list) -> bool:
    return any(action in user_events for action in AHA_MOMENT_ACTIONS)
```

## 10. Category Creation

Creating a new category rather than competing in an existing one is the highest-leverage strategic move for startups. It's also the hardest.

### When to Create a Category

Create a category when:
- Existing categories require too many caveats to describe your product
- The alternative you're disrupting has a toxic/boring brand (IT software, enterprise tools)
- You can be the definitive leader of the new category

### Category Creation Playbook

1. **Name the enemy** — the current way of doing things, not a competitor
2. **Name the category** — invent the term customers will use to search for you
3. **Define the problem** — create the problem frame in your terms
4. **Rally early adopters** — evangelize through thought leadership, conferences
5. **Educate the market** — blog, reports, community events
6. **Own the metrics** — create the KPIs the category gets measured by

**Famous examples:**
- Salesforce: "End of Software" → created "CRM" in customer minds
- HubSpot: named "Inbound Marketing" category
- Drift: named "Conversational Marketing"
- Intercom: "Messaging as business tool"

## 11. Competitive Strategy

### Porter's Five Forces (AI Era Update)

| Force | Traditional | AI Era Modification |
|---|---|---|
| **Supplier power** | Raw materials, talent | GPU compute (NVIDIA), foundation model APIs |
| **Buyer power** | Large customers | Low switching cost if AI UX is similar |
| **New entrants** | Capital, expertise | Low code barrier; high model/data barrier |
| **Substitutes** | Alternative products | AI commoditizes features faster than ever |
| **Rivalry** | Competitors | Race-to-zero on feature parity; differentiation required |

### Competitive Response Playbook

**If a large player copies your feature:**
- Move up-market to enterprise (more complex, harder to replicate)
- Move faster to your next moat (proprietary data, integrations)
- Deepen existing customer relationships (switching costs)
- Niche down further (serve the 20% they can't serve efficiently)

**If a well-funded startup enters your space:**
- Compete on customer success metrics, not features
- Lock in annual contracts before they finish GTM
- Win on implementation speed and customer outcomes

## 12. Strategic Planning Cadence

**Quarterly planning framework (OKR-based):**

```
Q-level Objective: "Become the default AI writing tool for legal"

Key Results:
  KR1: Reach $2M ARR from legal segment (from $800K)
  KR2: Achieve NPS >70 among legal customers
  KR3: 15 logo wins from AmLaw 200

Initiatives (from strategy):
  1. Launch Legal Pro tier with compliance features
  2. Hire 2 legal enterprise reps
  3. Publish "State of AI in Law" annual report
  4. Integration with Clio and PracticePanther

Weekly check: KR progress, blockers
Monthly review: Reforecast, resource reallocation
Quarterly: Full OKR review, new quarter plan
```

**Annual strategy process:**
1. **External scan** (Month 11): market shifts, competitor moves, customer feedback synthesis
2. **Internal assessment** (Month 11): SWOT, which bets worked, what failed
3. **Strategy choices** (Month 12): 3–5 bets for the year (not a wish list)
4. **Annual plan** (Month 12): headcount, budget, OKRs cascaded from strategy
5. **Board approval** (January): present plan, get alignment
